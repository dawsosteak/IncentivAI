# Office of Wellness and Resilience Home Page
## Office of Wellness and Resilience
The kuleana (responsibility; privilege) of the Office of Wellness and Resilience is to strengthen our state service systems, using hope-centered principles to create a trauma-informed state. We break down barriers that impact the well-being of Hawai‘i’s people – from keiki to kūpuna.

#### The 2026 Hawai’i Quality of Life Survey is now LIVE!
**Share Your Voice, Shape Hawai** ‘ _**i’s Future**_
In partnership with the Health Policy Initiative at the University of Hawai’i at Mānoa, we’re conducting the Hawai‘i Quality of Life Survey again to update the data and strengthen the decisions that matter most to you, whether in your community, workplace, or for state-level efforts. By sharing your experiences, you will help improve the well-being of Hawai‘i’s people today and for future generations. This anonymous, 10-20 minute survey is open to Hawaiʻi residents 18+ years old.
Broad participation is key to ensuring the survey reflects the diverse experiences of communities across Hawai‘i so please share far and wide.

**SUPPORTING YOURSELF THROUGH GRIEF**
U N D E R S T A N D I N G & C O P I N G W I T H G R I E F Grief is one of life's most universal experiences—a natural and necessary response to loss that connects us to our shared humanity. While deeply challenging, grief reflects the depth of our connections and the meaning we find in what we've lost. No two people grieve the same way, and you may find you rself responding differently to different losses throughout your life. There's no ti meline to follow, no "right" way to feel, and no need to compare your experience to others. The grieving process isn’t linear; it comes in waves. You might feel overwhelmed one moment and surprisingly okay the next. These shifts are completely normal and don't mean you're "getting over" your loss or that your grie f isn't real.No two people grieve the same way, and you may find you rself responding differently to different losses throughout your life. There's no ti meline to follow, no "right" way to feel, and no need to compare your experience to others. The grieving process isn’t linear; it comes in waves. You might feel overwhelmed one moment and surprisingly okay the next. These shifts are completely normal and don't mean you're "getting over" your loss or that your grie f isn't real. Grief can affect your whole self—emotionally, physically, and behaviorally. While it looks differently for everyone, some common experiences include: Emotional responses: disbelief, sadness, anger, guilt, confusion, helplessness, or irritability Physical responses: fatigue, changes in sleep or appetite, headaches, or frequent tears Behavioral changes: withdrawing from others, difficulty concentrating, reduced productivity, or changes in substance use Honor your needs. Prioritize rest, nourishing food, and gentle movement when possible. Create a simple list of activities that usually bring you comfort or joy, and try to include small doses of these regularly. Stay present with your emotions. Lean on connection. Spend time with people who you care about, even if it's just taking a drive or sharing a meal for a quick five minutes. Recognize when you need additional support. If grief begins significantly interfering with daily life, work, or relationships (especially after 6 months or more), reaching out to an employee assistance program (www.worklifehawaii.org), counselor, or healthcare provider can provide valuable guidance and support. Be gentle with yourself and others.
