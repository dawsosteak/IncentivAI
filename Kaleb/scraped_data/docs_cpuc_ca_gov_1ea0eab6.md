I. INTRODUCTION Cohen Ventures, Inc. dba Energy Solutions respectfully submits these comments on the Administrative Law Judge’s Ruling Providing Notice and Opportunity to Comment on Staff Proposal for Policy on Natural Gas Energy Efficiency Incentives issued December 1, 2025 (Ruling). Energy Solutions is the program administrator and implementer for TECH Clean California (“TECH Initiative”), a statewide market transformation program driving adoption of heat pump space and water heating technologies and submits these comments on behalf of the TECH Initiative Team. II. RESPONSE SUMMARY Calculating the costs and benefits of fuel substitution programs is significantly more complex and nuanced than “straight” energy efficiency. A customer’s payback period on the adoption of an electric alternative can be highly variable and dependent on many factors that are

RESPONSES TO SPECIFIC QUESTIONS The TECH Initiative Team offers responses to select questions posed in the Staff Proposal in which our program data or insights may be helpful. Equity: What other actions should this staff proposal take to encourage electrification among equity customers? The customer economics of using heat pumps relative to gas alternatives varies considerably based on an array of regional, technology, and customer-specific factors. Low income customers are more sensitive to the utility bill impacts of building electrification than wealthier customers. In general, equity programs should preferentially serve those customers who will benefit financially (though other benefits, such as resilience to extreme heat or indoor air quality, are key considerations). Incorporating the key drivers of customer bill savings into the Commission’s Customer Electrification Estimator Tool ideally would help ensure that

An important consideration is the valuation of customer and societal co-benefits, such as extreme heat resilience due to the added cooling functionality of an air-source heat pump replacing a furnace in a home without pre-existing air conditioning, or indoor air quality impacts. While the Participant Cost Test is a valuable measure of a customer’s willingness to pay for a specific measure, it does not account for the full value – to the customer, to ratepayers, or to society – of building electrification measures. Incorporating these benefits – such as health outcomes for fully decarbonized projects – into program-level cost effectiveness metrics would enable more building decarbonization projects in equity communities in which these issues are more acute. Is it safe to assume that the basic costs for connecting a fuel substitution measure to a building (wiring, labor) is currently included in each fuel substitution measure package in the eTRM? In the TECH Initiative Team’s experience, electric work (wiring, labor) that could be considered “basic” can still vary substantially depending on the specifics of the house and project. There is a substantial variety of electrical work required for the installation of fuel substitution measures, due to huge variations in California’s housing stock. The question’s examples of “wiring and labor” as “basic costs” leaves room for how much wiring and labor counts as “basic costs.” The work required for converting a gas furnace to a heat pump can vary greatly depending on where the existing furnace is located within a building in relation to where the new air handlers need to be installed, along with electrical reach to new outdoor units. This work can include moving circuits, adding circuits, running wiring around the side of the house (or through the attic), drywall repair, replacement of registers and ductwork, etc. The eTRM would likely benefit from explicit definitions of what “basic costs” entail with consistency across fuel substitution measures.

The analysis found the largest indicators of customer bill impacts from fuel substitution measures included: measure type, customer rate structure (customers on a CARE / FERA rate experienced significantly more bill savings from fuel substitution given the higher discount on electric rates compared to gas rates), climate zone (southern-most and central climate zones more correlated to bill savings) and if the customer was on a net-metering rate (correlated in greater bill savings) or TOU rate (correlated with bill increases). Should VEA measures use TRC or PCT for evaluating cost effectiveness? It is clear the Commission would like to avoid a scenario in which there is a Viable Electric Alternative “on paper,” but in which customers still encounter barriers to fuel substitution that surmount even incentivized electric equipment and, in the absence of incentives for high-efficiency gas equipment, resort to “baseline” gas equipment in practice. A survey of post-install survey of TECH Initiative participants10 found that the top consideration in customer decision-making to purchase a heat pump was finances, followed by a desire to complement existing or future solar installations by converting appliances to electric (which also has substantial financial considerations for ongoing cost). Therefore, in order to be “viable” from a customer perspective, an incentivized electric measure should have minimal incremental cost over the “baseline” measure, on a net present value basis. This approach aligns with the Participant Cost Test. Alternatively, using the Total Resource Cost (TRC) test includes many benefits that a customer may not necessarily value; measures that are cost-effective from a TRC perspective may not be cost-effective from a PCT perspective. In this situation, if the measure is still not “viable” from a customer perspective, the customer is more likely to not participate in programs at all. Notably, while the PCT does represent a potentially more accurate accounting of viability from the customer perspective than TRC, meeting the customer's threshold financial criteria is a necessary but not sufficient condition for “viability.” Barriers to fuel substitution measures are not solely financial. A customer choosing to pursue more than just a like-for-like replacement is likely to encounter barriers besides the upfront cost of the equipment, including the potential

There are many participant benefits of residential electrification (through fuel switching and substitution with heat pump adoptions), which extend beyond upfront costs and long-term operating costs. Those benefits include factors such as increased customer comfort, reduced noise (for HP HVAC), housing and property values, added functionality (i.e., a HP HVAC system providing cooling in addition to heating, which can reduce mortality and morbidity due to extreme heat).

VEA methodology and Assessment Do you agree with the proposed VEA methodology? If not, what should be added or changed? The proposed VEA methodology is tightly aligned with the deemed-measure approval process, and relies on detailed measure package definitions, offer IDs, high-level screens, and permutation-level analysis. Many building electrification measures are still not widespread, with the market (and technologies) continuing to evolve rapidly. The proposed VEA Methodology relies on building electrification data, such as pricing and bill impacts data, which should be updated frequently to reflect a progressively more detailed understanding of the evolving building electrification landscape. The TECH Initiative Team encourages the Commission to update and calibrate the key components of the VEA methodology, most notably including the Customer Electrification Estimator Tool with real meter and bill impact data at every point that new customer bill data is released by the CPUC or by a CPUC program. Ensuring the accuracy of estimates around operating cost and utility bill impacts for participants – including updating assumptions and methodology regularly to integrate most recent bill impact studies, program data, and utility tariffs – will be critical in ensuring that Viable Electric Alternatives are deployed in the most optimal way. The costs and benefits of building decarbonization vary substantially across the state. In order to maximize ratepayer benefits from building decarbonization investments, the cost effectiveness metrics (and program designs) would benefit from incorporating the key variables
