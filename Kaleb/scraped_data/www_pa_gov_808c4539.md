**Objectives**
2 • Identify common issues encountered by Pennsylvania Department of Health (DOH) Infection Control (IC) Plan reviewers • Understand solutions to move IC Plans through the approval process in a timely manner

4 T I T L E Infection Control Plan Title Information Include the document name (e.g., Infection Control Plan). Avoid using facility nicknames or legal names (if different from the working name). Example: Legal Name Smith County Surgery Center, LLC Working Name Surgery Center at Smith Place Include the calendar or fiscal year that the plan represents. Include the date of Infection Control Committee (ICC) approval for your IC Plan (can be “pending” if ICC has not yet approved).
* * *
5 L E N G T H Find the Goldilocks Zone There are no length requirements for an IC Plan. However, a review of the length can provide insight into the edits that may be required for approval. The average length of approved IC Plans is 15-25 pages in length. Too Long Copied information from infection control policies and procedures, the law, or Pennsylvania code. Too Short Missing elements from the Medical Care Availability and Reduction of Error (MCARE) Act and other applicable laws. Just Right Utilized the facility specific outline resource from the DOH Infection Control Plan Submission website.
* * *
6 L E N G T H Too Long • Copying and pasting information from existing policies or codes is redundant. – Creates opportunity for misalignment between policies and the IC plan. • Summarize your program while still capturing the elements required by MCARE and other applicable laws.
* * *
7 L E N G T H Too Short • Lacks the content required to meet the specific details in MCARE and other applicable laws.
* * *
8 L E N G T H MCARE and Other Applicable Laws Federal Regulations • Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) • Federal Conditions of Participation for Hospitals • Conditions for Coverage for Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs) • Requirements for States and Long-Term Care Facilities (LTCFs) State Regulations • MCARE • Health Care Facilities Act (HCFA) • Pennsylvania Code for Reportable Diseases, Hospitals, ASCs, and LTCFs Other Regulations • County and local public health rules and regulations • Current nationally recognized guidelines and evidence-based practices relevant to the facility
* * *
9 L E N G T H Just Right • Finding the Goldilocks zone is often the result of: – Using the IC Plan development resources from the DOH website. – Engaging with the IC Plan reviewers via email or by attending Wednesday
* * *
> “office hours” using the meeting information from the DOH website.
10 G U I D E Using Available Resources • If you are using a recently approved IC Plan that’s been shared from another facility: – Review the entire plan to assure all existing content is revised to reflect services offered and practices used at your facility. • If you are using the IC Plan development resources from the DOH website: – Outlines are provided for each facility type (i.e., ASCs, hospitals, LTCFs), but each requires heavy editing to be facility specific. Review the key concepts at the beginning prior to utilizing this resource.
* * *
11 T E R M I N O L O G Y Attention to Language The IC Plan should reflect the most current terminology being referenced in the industry. For example: • Health care Associated Infections (HAI) instead of Hospital Acquired Infections • Hand Hygiene instead of handwashing • Standard Precautions instead of Universal Precautions • Patient Safety Authority’s rolling online journal articles/advisories and annual Patient Safety Journal instead of Patient Safety Authority Journal • Patient Safety Authority’s “Training Manual and User’s Guide Nursing Home Event Reporting” (adaptation of updated McGeer Criteria and CDC guidelines) instead of revised McGeer Criteria
* * *
12 T E R M I N O L O G Y Acronyms If acronyms are utilized, they should be clearly defined the first time the term is introduced. For example: • Health care Associated Infections (HAI) • Patient Safety Authority (PSA) • Certification in Infection Control (CIC) – This refers to the credential issued by the Certification Board of Infection Control and Epidemiology after passing a standardized (proctored) test.
* * *
13 R I S K A S S E S S M E N T Correct Language • Risks should be worded as risks rather than expectations. For example: – Inadequate performance of hand hygiene instead of hand hygiene – Lack of compliance with mandatory education instead of staff completing education upon hire – Improper transport of linen instead of adherence to linen policies • Risk assessments should include at least 1 HAI-related risk.
* * *
14 R I S K A S S E S S M E N T Appropriate Scoring • Probability of occurrence scores should never be zero. – If there is zero probability of a risk occurring at your facility, it does not need to be listed on your current risk assessment. • Risk harm or level of failure scores should never be zero. – If an event is truly a risk, the potential for harm always exists. Scores should be based on the level of harm that could occur if the event happened, not the current potential for harm in the facility.
* * *
15 R I S K A S S E S S M E N T Facility Specific • No two risk assessments should be alike. – There could be duplication in the risk events listed, but scoring should always be facility specific. Once risk levels are totaled, the highest scoring risks should have corresponding goals to show how the facility is attempting to mitigate those risks. • Resources to assist you in making a facility specific risk assessment include: – DOH Resources – University of North Carolina SPICE resource for LTC
* * *
16 G O A L S Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound (SMART) • Goals should be SMART, facility specific, and related to the highest priority risks from your risk assessment. • Goals can be captured in a separate worksheet/tab within the risk assessment, within the IC Plan itself or within an appendix of the IC Plan.
* * *
17 G O A L S Worksheet Format Example • For this example, assume the risk event listed below had a total risk score that met or exceeded the threshold set by the facility for determining high priority risks. Risk Event Measurable Goal Strategies for Improvement Responsible Party Annual Outcome Lack of annual N95 fit testing 100% of staff will complete fit testing by December 2024 Send weekly reminders regarding fit testing. Offer additional opportunities including during annual influenza clinic. Follow-up with any employees that have incomplete fit testing by requiring completion prior to start of next working shift. Infection Preventionist and Nurse Educator • At the end of the specified timeframe, the goal is reviewed, and any relevant information is captured under annual outcome. If a goal was not achieved and the event still presents a risk in the facility, strategies should be revised and a new timeline set.
* * *
18 B E S T P R A C T I C E S Consistency Choose a single term and/or abbreviation and use it consistently in the document. Example variations: • Preventionist – IP, IP professional, ICP, IC officer, IC nurse • Plan – ICP, IC plan, IP plan, IPCP • Program – IP, IPCP, IP program, IC program • Department – IP department, IC department, IPC department • Committees – IC Committee, ICC, IP&C; Committee, QA Committee, QAPI
* * *
19 Summary Make documents specific to your facility Use appropriate resources Be consistent with abbreviations Align terminology with current industry standards Support your risk assessment with SMART goals

**BACKGROUND**
**IDENTIFYING INFECTION RISK EVENTS**
Date updated: January 2026 1 Instructions for Using the Infection Prevention and Control Risk Assessment Resource An infection prevention and control (IPC) risk assessment (RA) is a proactive way to help prevent infections by systematically identifying and assessing risks for acquiring or transmitting infections in a specific health care facility. It allows facilities to prioritize risks and develop action plans to mitigate or eliminate infection risks. An IPC RA is performed by the facility’s infection preventionist (IP) with input from the infection control committee. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) stipulates that an IPC RA is completed annually and when needs arise, (e.g., a new service line, a novel infection outbreak, changes in practice standards or regulations) according to the following regulations for Acute Care Hospitals (ACH), Long-Term Care Facilities (LTC), and Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASC). The IPC RA is NOT an: • Emergency Management or Environment of Care all-hazards Risk Assessment or Hazard Vulnerability Assessment (HVA). These tools are used to identify potential hazards that impact a facility’s emergency management. • Infection Control Risk Assessment (ICRA), which assesses infection risks associated with construction or remodeling. • Infection Control Assessment and Response (ICAR), which is used to assess how well a facility is implementing IPC methodologies. The ICAR tool can inform an IPC RA but does not replace it. This IPC RA Resource was created by the Pennsylvania Department of Health (DOH), Bureau of Epidemiology Healthcare Associated Infection Prevention (HAIP) Division to help PA health care facilities develop facility-specific IPC RAs. An IPC RA is a required document for IC plan submissions to the DOH, however the use of this resource is optional. Infection risk events are anything that could cause infectious disease related harm (e.g., injury or death of patients, residents, staff, loss of business revenue or facility reputation, service interruption or damage to the property and facility). Infection risk events must also be detectable and preventable through available policies, procedures, resources, supplies, and training. Sources of risk events include surveillance data, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) Core Infection Prevention and Control Practices for Safe Health care Delivery in All Settings, ICAR tools, rounding observations, etc. Consider infection risk events related to the following groups: • Administrative (e.g., lack of leadership support of the IPC program, no qualified IP on staff, high proportion of contracted agency staff) • Population served (e.g., risks related to age, immune status, and other patient characteristics) • Improper IC practice by services offered (e.g., procedure types [especially low volume, high complexity], vendor supplied services in LTC, nursing units, service lines) • Community-based risks (e.g., geography, weather, natural disasters, proximity to an international border or port of entry, multidrug-resistant organism prevalence/incidence) • Environment of Care (EOC) (e.g., age of structure and utility systems, construction, renovation processes, utility outages) • Staff competency (e.g., lack of competency-based training programs) • Occupational Safety or Employee Health risks (e.g., missed new hire health screenings, lack of compliance with vaccine programs, exposures, outbreaks, non-compliance with Occupational Safety and Health Administration programs)

**POPULATING THE IPC RA RESOURCE**
Date updated: January 2026 2 Instructions for Using the Infection Prevention and Control Risk Assessment Resource • IC practice (e.g., staff non-compliance with current evidence-based practice for standard precautions, transmission-based precautions, enhanced barrier precautions, health care-associated infection (HAI) prevention bundles) • Medical devices (e.g., contaminated temporary and permanent invasive devices and reusable medical equipment, re-use of single-use items, contamination in devices with water reservoirs, cleaning, high-level disinfection, and sterilization failure) • Health care-associated infections Consistently label risk events in the negative (e.g., Hand hygiene non-compliance or Lack of hand hygiene), NOT as an expected practice (e.g., Hand hygiene). This ensures that tallied scores only reflect risks. An IPC RA Resource is available on the DOH website (link). It is designed to help you create an IPC RA unique to your facility. The Risk Assessment tabs of the resource contain facility-type specific IPC RAs. Some general risk events have been pre-populated, but they may not cover everything for your facility. Add and delete rows to align with your facility’s infection risks. Score each risk event according to the Probability or Frequency of Occurrence (Column B), Impact (Column C), and your facility’s Preparedness (Column D). These measures are defined below. • PROBABILITY or FREQUENCY OF OCCURRENCE: How frequently does or could the risk event occur? o Review historical data o Range: 0 to 3  0 – Very low: Refers to infrequent events like earthquakes.  1 – Low: Refers to events occurring roughly annually, like winter storms.  2 – Medium: Refers to events occurring roughly monthly to multiple times a year, like staff turnover or HAIs.  3 – High: Refers to events occurring weekly to daily like hand hygiene non-compliance. • IMPACT (Human and interruption of care): How severely is the event expected to impact the facility’s patients or residents and health care personnel) o Score according to the most probable occurrence, not the worst-case scenario. o Range: 0 to 3  0 – Very Low: No injury or harm.  1 – Low: Requires monitoring and no treatment. Minimal interruption of care.  2 – Medium: Requires treatment, prophylaxis, or increased level of care. Some interruption of care.  3 – High: Loss of life or limb. Significant interruption of care. • FACILITY PREPAREDNESS (Prevention and mitigation measures): How well-prepared is the facility (staff and resources) to prevent the risk events from occurring and/or reduce the severity of the event if it occurs? o This includes things such as the ability to detect the event, existing policies and procedures, staff compliance, staff training, and staff access to available resources (e.g., equipment, policy, procedures, subject-matter expert). o Range: 3 to 0

**ANNUAL IPC PROGRAM GOALS**
Date updated: January 2026 3 Instructions for Using the Infection Prevention and Control Risk Assessment Resource  3 – Poor: Incorrect, inaccurate, or non-existent policies, procedures, education, and training for staff. Requires a lot of external assistance or is an all-hands-on-deck situation.  2 – Fair: Policies and procedures exist but no staff training. Will need external assistance to stay operational.  1 – Good: Staff are trained, but there is poor compliance. Minimal to no external assistance needed to stay operational.  0 – Excellent: Staff is well versed with prevention and response measures. The score for each risk event is the sum of Probability or Frequency of Occurrence, Impact, and Facility Preparedness. The score will range from 0 (lowest vulnerability) to 9 (highest vulnerability). The IPC RA Resource will calculate the score automatically if numerical values are entered in columns B, C, and D. Develop goals to mitigate the highest scoring events on the IPC RA. The goals may be recorded in the IPC Program Goals tab of the IPC Risk Assessment Resource or included in the IC plan. Lower-scoring risk events may be selected because of desired facility focus or regulatory requirements, e.g., hand hygiene compliance, bloodborne pathogen exposures, and CMS reportable HAIs. When developing annual IPC program goals consider the following: • Goals must mitigate identified risks using nationally recognized and evidence-based standards, guidelines, and IC practices. • Develop SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time bound), e.g., “75% hand hygiene compliance from monthly observations in all departments by Q4 2025,” instead of “Improve hand hygiene in the facility.” • Identify the people and departments that will be responsible for the work of meeting each goal; it should not all be the responsibility of the IP. • Develop clear criteria to monitor progress and assess for improvement. • Revise IPC program policies and procedures when needed to align with new risk mitigation strategies planned for meeting annual goals.
