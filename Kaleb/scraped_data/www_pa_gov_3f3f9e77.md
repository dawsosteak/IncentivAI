## The .gov means it's official.

# Interactive Data Reports
These tools give you an interactive look at controlled substance prescribing and drug overdose trends at the state and county level, and prescribing trends by provider category at the state level.  

## How to Use
To get started, navigate to the dashboard you are interested in by using the buttons below. Each dashboard is interactive and includes using drop-down boxes or more information viewable by moving your mouse over the data visualizations. The data visualizations will automatically reflect the options you select. Reports with maps allow you to zoom in and out, as well as scroll through the different time periods to see changes over time.
## Technical Notes
The emergency department overdose data presented represents data collected through syndromic surveillance. Syndromic surveillance provides public health officials with a near real-time system for detecting, understanding, and monitoring health events. The Department utilizes portions of de-identified emergency department record data to identify suspected overdoses. These cases are not individually investigated. Additionally, not all overdose victims go to an emergency department. The overdose death data is compiled through death records from the Bureau of Health Statistics and Registries, as well as coroner and medical examiner records from participating counties. Please see [Overdose Data Technical Notes(opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/opioids/OverdoseDataTechnicalNotes.pdf) for more information on how emergency department and death data was compiled.
Prescribing data represents data submitted to the Pennsylvania PDMP by pharmacies and dispensing practitioners. Please see [PDMP Data Technical Notes(opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/programs/pdmp/PDMPDataTechnicalNotes.pdf) for more information on how this data was compiled.
Prescribing data by category uses a dataset that combines PDMP controlled substance data and provider classification data from the Center for Medicaid and Medicare Services (CMS). Please see the [PDMP Data by Prescriber Category Technical Notes(opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/health/documents/topics/documents/programs/pdmp/PDMP%20Data%20by%20Prescriber%20Category%20Technical%20Notes.pdf) for more information on how this data was compiled.

* * *
As required by the Overdose Mapping Act of November 3, 2022 P.L. 2158, No. 158 (Act), the Pennsylvania Department of Health (PA DOH), in conjunction with Pennsylvania State Police (PSP), conducted its annual report of the overdose responses and naloxone administrations by Pennsylvania criminal justice agencies for the calendar year 2023.
* * *
Methods
* * *
Using data from the Overdose Information Network (ODIN) and PA DOH, the DOH analyzed the impact of overdoses on each county, which included the total number of fatal and nonfatal overdoses, the total number of overdose reversal drug administered by law enforcement, the total number of opioid overdoses, the total number of overdoses not containing opioids, and the total number of overdoses containing fentanyl.

OVERDOSE MAPPING ACT ANNUAL REPORT – 2023 PENNSYLVANIA DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH 5 Background
* * *
As required by the Overdose Mapping Act of November 3, 2022 P.L. 2158, No. 158 (Act), the Pennsylvania Department of Health (PA DOH), in conjunction with Pennsylvania State Police (PSP), conducted its annual report of the overdose responses and naloxone administrations by Pennsylvania criminal justice agencies from January 1, 2023, to December 31, 2023. The Overdose Mapping Act was established to provide a platform, Overdose Information Network (ODIN) for law enforcement agencies within the state to disseminate data on drug overdoses in near real-time, aiming to improve overdose response, and assisting in reducing the effects of the overdose epidemic. Pennsylvania had the fourth highest number of fatal drug overdoses in the country in 20221.
* * *
Methods PSP completed participation agreements with Commonwealth, county, and local law enforcement agencies during initial Pennsylvania Justice Network (JNET) registration for the ODIN system, which allows for data sharing. Law enforcement officers reported the date and time for suspected overdoses, as well as the location of the overdose, and the known or suspected controlled substances used by the person who overdosed into ODIN.
* * *
The PA DOH collects a variety of data on fatal and nonfatal overdoses. Fatal overdose data includes death records from the Bureau of Health Statistics and Registries (BHSR) at the PA DOH and toxicology and coroner/medical examiner reports through partnerships with 55 individual county coroners/medical examiners and the Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs. The BHSR uses a modified version of the Council of State and Territorial Epidemiologists2 Epi Tool to Analyze Overdose Death Data to pull monthly data extracts for the DOH’s Office of Drug Surveillance and Misuse Prevention (ODSMP). Drug overdoses are then further identified by the PA DOH’s ODSMP using ICD-10 codes for accidental drug poisonings (X40-X44) and drug poisoning of undetermined intent (Y10-Y14) as well as manual reviews of death certificates and coroner/medical examiner reports.
* * *
Suspected nonfatal overdoses are identified via the PA DOH’s vendor-hosted syndromic surveillance system, EpiCenter, which receives portions of the electronic health record for all emergency department visits from non-federal emergency departments in the state. In 2023, 171/173 (99%) emergency departments were connected to EpiCenter. PA DOH’s EpiCenter vendor, Health Monitoring, uses keyword searches and natural language processing, and International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes to categorize suspected overdose related visits into a variety of categories, including: Any Drug overdoses, which includes poisonings from any illicit, prescription or over-the-counter substances; Any Opioid overdoses, including poisonings from any suspected illicit or prescription opioids; and

Limitations This report has several limitations. First, PSP was conducting onboarding of law enforcement agencies into the use of the ODIN system during 2023, which may lead to underestimation of the true number of overdoses where law enforcement was present. Fatal overdoses identified in ODIN do not necessarily represent the official cause of death as determined by a coroner or medical examiner. PSP drug-specific information is not based on comprehensive toxicological testing and is most often representative of what substance(s) the reporter suspected the victim was using. Fatal and nonfatal overdoses are not reportable conditions in Pennsylvania, thus DOH monitors fatal and nonfatal overdoses via death certificates and syndromic surveillance, respectively and suspected overdoses identified by syndromic surveillance are not investigated by public health staff for case ascertainment purposes. Syndromic surveillance is based on the chief complaint, then additionally based on diagnosis codes, which are an optional submission component. The chief complaint is often not specific and can lead to underestimating the number of drug-specific nonfatal overdoses. Nonfatal overdoses that are seen in emergency departments often do not receive comprehensive toxicological testing, which can also underestimate the number of overdoses when looking at drug-specific trends. Additional details about the DOH’s fatal and nonfatal overdose surveillance can be found in the Overdose Data Technical Notes2. Finally, each data source referenced in this report uses a different location on which the data are aggregated; ODIN overdose data are based on the incident location, EpiCenter data are based on the residence location of the overdose victim, PA DOH fatal overdose death location is based on the county of death. Since the locations used for data aggregation vary, direct comparison of each data source to the others will never yield identical results.
* * *
Conclusions The overdose epidemic continues to impact Pennsylvanians statewide. However, the data show that some counties have higher overdose death rates than others and the type of substance contributing to the death varies by geographic region. Continued use of ODIN and increasing the user base so there are more active ODIN reporters among law enforcement personnel may assist in disseminating crucial information to lower the number of fatal drug overdoses.
