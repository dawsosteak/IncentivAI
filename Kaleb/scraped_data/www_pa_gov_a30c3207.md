# Fact-Checking Pennsylvania-Related Election Claims
We are committed to providing accurate, trustworthy information about election administration in Pennsylvania and to combatting damaging misinformation and disinformation about our elections. 
## Voting by mail is safe and secure, and no evidence exists of widespread mail voting fraud in Pennsylvania.   

**The short answer**
Mail ballot fraud has been proven to be exceptionally rare. Claims of systemic voter fraud are devoid of any supporting evidence and have consistently been rejected by judges, government agencies, and election experts across the political spectrum.  

###  See the full answer
Mail voting in Pennsylvania has multiple layers of security that involve registered voters having to provide appropriate ID before their mail ballot can be counted and that prevent any voter from casting more than one ballot in any given election.
Pennsylvanians have been voting by mail since the Civil War, and the process has a long history of being safe and secure.
## Pennsylvania’s mail ballots are not “emergency backup ballots.” Requesting and returning a mail ballot will make a voter ineligible to vote by regular ballot at the polls. If an emergency occurs on Election Day that prevents a voter from reporting to the polling place, the voter can apply for an [emergency absentee ballot](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-support/mail-in-and-absentee-ballot/emergency-absentee-ballot), which can be picked up by the voter or by an authorized representative. 
**The short answer**
Mail ballots are available to all eligible Pennsylvanians and should be used when the voter does not plan to vote in person at their polling place on Election Day. They are not “emergency backup ballots.” If a voter plans to vote in person, they should not request a mail ballot. Returning a mail ballot prior to Election Day will create a record in the system that prevents a voter from casting a regular ballot on Election Day, as each voter may only vote once. Voters who request a mail ballot and do not complete it will be required to vote provisionally if they report to their polling place on Election Day unless they bring their mail ballot and return envelope with them to be spoiled by election workers at the polling place. Emergency absentee ballots do exist, but are distinct. Emergency absentee ballots can be used by a person who did not apply for a mail ballot by the standard application deadline (seven days before Election Day) if an emergency arises after that deadline but before Election Day that prevents the person from getting to the polls. The applicant must attest to the reason why the application is an emergency. 
###  See the full answer
Mail ballots allow a voter to receive their ballot prior to Election Day, fill it out, and return it by mail, directly to their county elections office, or at another site authorized by the county. All registered Pennsylvania voters have the option to vote by mail. For the 2025 General Election, the deadline to apply for a mail ballot is 5 pm on October 28. The county elections office must receive the completed ballot by 8 pm on November 4 (Election Day). Voters may apply for a mail ballot online or via paper application submitted to their county elections office by mail or in person. Some counties have set up satellite county offices that provide voters with more options to apply for, receive, and vote their mail ballot. Mail ballots should be returned at the voter’s earliest possible convenience to avoid delivery after Election Day, at which point the ballot cannot be counted. 
If a voter requests a mail ballot and returns it, they cannot vote a regular ballot at the polls on Election Day. If the voter has been sent a mail ballot, they will have to vote provisionally unless they bring the ballot and the return envelope to the polls to be spoiled. In no event may a voter cast more than one ballot, and safeguards in the Pennsylvania system will prevent them from doing so. If a voter plans to vote in person, using a regular mail ballot as a “backup” may result in having to vote provisionally, and the provisional ballot will not be counted if the mail ballot is counted by the county. Requesting a mail ballot with an intention to vote at the polls creates additional work for county poll workers which will ultimately cause longer lines at the polls. Any person applying for a mail ballot at least seven days before Election Day will be issued a standard mail-in or absentee ballot.
An emergency absentee ballot can only be requested in certain limited circumstances involving last-minute emergencies that occur after the mail ballot application deadline (these can include an unexpected illness or a last-minute absence from a voter's municipality). To obtain an emergency ballot, a voter must request one between 5 pm on the Tuesday before the election (the mail ballot deadline) and 8 pm on Election Day. The application forms, including one for an authorized representative to pick up an emergency ballot, can be found on the [Pennsylvania Department of State’s website](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-support/mail-in-and-absentee-ballot/emergency-absentee-ballot). This type of ballot exists to ensure that an unexpected occurrence shortly before or on Election Day does not prevent a voter from casting their ballot. 
## **Pennsylvania’s database of registered voters undergoes continuous, year-round updates that follow state and federal law and help ensure our voter rolls are accurate.**
**The short answer**
Pennsylvania’s 67 county elections offices maintain Pennsylvania’s voter registration rolls through a centralized database known as the SURE system (Statewide Uniform Registry of Electors). County officials follow rigorous procedures dictated by federal and state law, and they use multiple reliable sources to ensure that voter lists are as accurate as possible while protecting voters from being wrongfully removed.
This process is transparent, and you can read about how many voters were cancelled or labelled “inactive” each year by reading [the Department’s Annual Reports on Voter Registration](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/dos/resources/voting-and-elections-resources/election-reports.html#accordion-ea923da40b-item-b01fb1423f).
###  See the full answer
County elections officials – not the Department of State – do the work of keeping our voter rolls up to date, which is a process known as voter list maintenance. Their work is carefully regulated by federal and state law.
It is important to remember that a voter list will never be 100% accurate at a given moment in time. That is because voters die, move, or update their registrations every day. Both federal and state law recognize this and set up a careful balance so that, on the one hand, voters who have died or moved can be removed from the voter rolls but, on the other hand, voters will not be wrongfully disenfranchised through improper cancellation of their registration.
County elections officials regularly review data sources such as postal service change of address notices, state death records, and obituaries to check for voters who have moved or died. The information they look for, and how it can be used, is also regulated by state and federal law.
For example, county elections officials receive regular death notices from the state Department of Health. Pennsylvania’s membership in the Electronic Registration Information Center ([ERIC](https://ericstates.org/about/)) ensures the Department of State is notified when a voter moves to a new state so the Department can notify the voter’s prior home county for further action.
Specifically, when voters appear on postal change of address notices, counties are legally required to contact them to verify whether their registration should be cancelled, updated, or kept the same. Many people may temporarily change their address – for reasons such as going away to college or working a temporary job – but intend to maintain their registration in Pennsylvania. The processes in place make sure these voters can stay registered at their home if they choose.
Also, when people move from one state to another, they often register to vote in their new state but don’t think to cancel their registration in their former state. Again, the procedures in place ensure the outdated registration is ultimately cancelled.
To learn more about voter list maintenance in Pennsylvania – including exploring historic data on voter registration cancellations – visit our [Election Reports ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/dos/resources/voting-and-elections-resources/election-reports.html#accordion-ea923da40b-item-b01fb1423f)page and scroll down to “Annual Reports on Voter Registration,” where you can view reports back to 1995.
## Pennsylvania’s automatic voter registration (AVR) only permits Pennsylvanians with documents proving their eligibility to register, and all eligible applicants are given the opportunity to opt out. Noncitizens are prohibited from accessing the AVR process, and no Pennsylvania resident is ever registered to vote without their consent.
**The short answer**
To be eligible to [register to vote](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-registration) in Pennsylvania, applicants must be: 
  * a U.S. citizen for at least 30 days, 


  * a resident of Pennsylvania for at least 30 days, and 


  * at least 18 years old on or before the date of the next election. 


When Pennsylvania residents go to Department of Transportation (PennDOT) centers to obtain a new or renewed state ID, only those residents who meet all three of the above criteria are presented with the automatic voter registration (AVR) screens. PennDot employees are required to put an indicator on the record of Individuals who may not be U.S. citizens so that those individuals are not presented with the AVR screens. Eligible applicants who are shown the screens must additionally affirm that they are U.S. citizens and are always presented with an opportunity to opt out of the AVR process. 
###  See the full answer
Pennsylvania’s AVR process has built-in protections that enable only U.S. citizens to apply to register to vote. 
When Pennsylvanians visit a PennDOT driver or photo license center to apply for a driver’s license or other state ID, they must provide documentation that proves their age, residency, and citizenship – all requirements needed to determine if a person is eligible to register to vote in Pennsylvania. 
If the applicant is at least 18 years old on or before the date of the next election, has been a resident of Pennsylvania for at least 30 days, and has been a U.S. citizen for at least 30 days, they are automatically presented with the AVR screens. The AVR screens also provide an opportunity to decline to apply for voter registration. 
Once an applicant goes through the AVR process, their application is sent to the elections office in the county where they live, and that county elections office once again reviews the applicant’s eligibility before the applicant is officially registered to vote. 
The transmission of voter applications from PennDOT to county election offices has been an option for Pennsylvanians since the 1993 passage of the [National Voter Registration Act](https://www.justice.gov/crt/national-voter-registration-act-1993-nvra), also known as the motor voter law. 
## Pennsylvania does not have early voting the way many other states do.  

**The short answer**
Pennsylvanians can [vote by mail ballot in person at their county elections office](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-support/mail-in-and-absentee-ballot/mail-ballot-before-election-day) or designated satellite office once ballots have been finalized and are available.
In Pennsylvania, registered voters can vote before Election Day only by completing and returning their mail-in ballot. Although voters can return their mail ballots up to and including Election Day, they had to apply for their mail ballot by 5 p.m. one week before Election Day. 
###  See the full answer
Voters have three ways to request their mail-in ballot:
  * [online](https://www.pavoterservices.pa.gov/OnlineAbsenteeApplication/#/OnlineMailInBegin), 
  * [by mail](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/vote/resources/documents-and-forms/PADOS_MailInApplication.pdf), or
  * in person at their [county election office](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/contact-us/contact-your-election-officials) (or other officially designated satellite office). With this option, a voter can request, receive, complete, and return their mail ballot all in one visit.


Although voters can return their completed ballot before Election Day, state law does not allow election officials to open those ballots and prepare them for counting – a process known as pre-canvassing – until 7 a.m. on Election Day. In addition, there are no polling places open for in-person voting before Election Day.
Therefore, Pennsylvania does not ever have “early voting” tallies to report. Results will begin being reported on our [Election Night Returns website](https://www.electionreturns.pa.gov/) after the polls close at 8 p.m. on Election Day.
County elections offices must begin processing mail ballot **applications** no later than 50 days before Election Day. That date is not a hard-and-fast deadline for when counties must have ballots available to provide to voters who request them.
##  _Poll workers_ are nonpartisan staff who run polling places on Election Day. They check in voters, assist with equipment, track turnout, and may deliver ballot materials after polls close. They cannot engage in partisan political activity. They may also be called election judges, inspectors, or clerks, or the district election board.
##  _Poll watchers_ are credentialed representatives of a candidate or political party. They are permitted to observe inside the polling place but cannot interfere with voting.
## Detailed information on what poll watchers can and cannot do is available on the Department’s [Guidance Concerning Poll Watchers and Authorized Representatives](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/dos/resources/voting-and-elections/directives-and-guidance/2025/guidance_poll%20watchers%20and%20authorized%20representatives_092225.pdf). 
**The short answer**
Except for voters in the process of voting their own ballot, only duly elected or appointed poll workers are allowed to handle ballots at the polling place. These poll workers swear an oath to "impartially and faithfully perform [their] duties" prior to serving in their role. Poll workers are prohibited from engaging in partisan political activity on Election Day.
Those who step up to serve in these important poll worker roles know firsthand that the process of voting in Pennsylvania is free, fair, safe, and secure. Pennsylvania voters can have confidence that their ballot will not be manipulated by those with partisan interests.
Poll watchers, on the other hand, are appointed by candidates appearing on the ballot. A poll watcher must:
  * be a qualified registered elector of the county where their appointed polling place is located; 


  * be identified and receive official county credentials in advance


###  See the full answer
_**Poll workers**_ are elected or appointed to their position and manage the polling place on Election Day. They help keep track of the number of voters, and ensure the ballot returns are delivered to the county election office after polls close. Poll workers swear an oath to “impartially and faithfully perform [their] duties.”
_**Poll watchers**_ are credentialed representatives of a candidate or political party who may be present inside the polling place during Election Day. They can make good-faith challenges to a voter’s identity or continued residence in the election district. Such challenges must be lodged directly with the Judge of Elections, and poll watchers have no authority to approach voters at the polling place or prevent a voter from voting.
In addition, poll watchers are not permitted to mark or in any way alter official election records, access the contents of ballot boxes, or interfere with the orderly process of voting.
The Election Code does not permit poll watchers and authorized representatives to handle ballots. ## **Pennsylvanians won’t always know the final results of all races on election night, and any changes in results that occur after election night are _not_ evidence that an election is “rigged.”**
**The short answer**
Hundreds of thousands – sometimes millions – of mail ballots are cast in every election, and **current state law does not permit counties to begin opening these ballots until 7 a.m. on Election Day. That means county election officials cannot even remove the ballots from the envelopes and prepare them to be scanned until that time** – on a day when those same officials are also running more than 9,000 polling places across the state.
Then, under the Election Code, counties may not even begin to record and publish mail ballot results until after the polls close at 8 p.m. Election Day.
###  See the full answer
While we would all like to go to bed on election night knowing who won in every race, determining [unofficial results](https://www.electionreturns.pa.gov/) is a days-long process because county elections officials must follow the careful, deliberative process and timeline prescribed by Pennsylvania’s Election Code to achieve a thorough count of every eligible vote.
An accurate count of all votes cast by eligible voters is paramount to our democracy and cannot be rushed. County election workers must be given a reasonable amount of time to do their jobs and follow the law.
Even after the initial rounds of unofficial vote tallies are recorded and published, county canvass boards must continue the elections work. Those boards must meet no later than 9 a.m. on the Friday after the election and must continue canvassing and counting through the eighth day after the election.
Among other work, these boards adjudicate provisional ballots — ballots that are cast when it’s unclear if the voter is in the correct polling place or when a voter who applied for a mail ballot decides to vote in person instead but doesn’t have their mail ballot to surrender.
It’s important to note that at every step — during [in-person voting at the polls](https://www.pa.gov/services/vote/find-your-local-polling-place), pre-canvassing and canvassing of [mail ballots](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-support/mail-in-and-absentee-ballot), and adjudication of [provisional ballots](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/vote/voter-support/provisional-ballot) — representatives selected by each candidate and political party may be present and observe the process to ensure the integrity of the vote count.
While news media “call” races on election night when there appears to be a wide and seemingly insurmountable margin between candidates, it’s important to note that they are basing their calls on unofficial results.
In close races, every vote must be counted before the outcome can be known. 
## Pennsylvania law requires that candidates’ names on general election ballots appear in a defined political party order – specifically, candidates in the same party as the current governor must be listed first.
**The short answer**
According to the Pennsylvania Election Code [25. P.S. § 2963(b)], candidates on general election ballots must be listed based on which party received the most votes in the last gubernatorial election. This means that, if the sitting governor is a Democrat, Democratic candidates in each race must be listed first; and if the sitting governor is a Republican, Republican candidates in each race must be listed first.
###  See the full answer
Since state law dictates the sequence of candidate names on the ballot, county and local officials cannot make any changes to the sequence based on personal preference.
The required order of candidate names remains in effect for four years, until Pennsylvanians again vote for governor.

## **Claims that there were “more votes than voters” in Pennsylvania in the 2020 general election are false.**
**The short answer**
Official public documents from the Department of State show that [6,945,045 registered voters successfully cast a ballot](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/dos/programs/voting-and-elections/2020-General-Election-Report.pdf) in Pennsylvania’s November 2020 election and that a total of [6,915,283 votes were recorded](https://www.archives.gov/files/electoral-college/2020/ascertainment-pennsylvania.pdf) as cast in the presidential election. These two data points clearly show that there were not “more votes than voters” in the 2020 general election.
###  See the full answer
The November 2020 election was free, fair, safe, and secure. Claims of systemic voter fraud are devoid of any supporting evidence and have consistently been rejected by judges, government agencies, and election experts across the political spectrum.   

On Nov. 24, 2020, the Pennsylvania Department of State officially certified the final results of the 2020 general election based on the certified results provided by each county as statutorily required.
Pennsylvania’s Election Code outlines the process for counties to verify the number of voters and the number of ballots cast in each election. That process, which takes place during the official canvass, involves county boards of elections comparing the number of registered voters against the certificates returned by each election district that show the number of people who voted and the number of ballots cast. 
There is no evidence that any county board of elections failed to complete this post-election requirement or identified a discrepancy during this process that could have impacted the outcome of any election, including the 2020 general election. 
Trying to extrapolate numbers from the Statewide Uniform Registry of Electors (SURE) system based on vote history contained in the [Full Voter Export](https://www.pavoterservices.pa.gov/pages/PurchasePAFullVoterExport.aspx?Langcode=en-US) (FVE) file is not an accurate way to reconcile votes cast to the number of voters who participated in any individual election. 
While the SURE system is an important tool for county election officials to record the vote history for every individual registered voter in the Commonwealth, it was never intended to – and should not be used to – crosscheck vote totals for any specific election. Voter records in SURE are constantly updated as new voters register, or registered voters move to a different county and have their record transferred, or voters have their record cancelled as a result of moving to a different state, death, or other cancellations pursuant to state and federal law, such as protracted inactivity. 
So, for example, a registered voter in Philadelphia County who voted in the November 2020 general election but moved to New Jersey the next day would have their voter record cancelled in the SURE system when Philadelphia’s Board of Elections receives confirmation that the voter has moved. In that case, the voter’s general information – and the record of their having voted in November 2020 – would no longer be listed in subsequent FVE files. 
The FVE is a snapshot of the moment in time it is downloaded and cannot, therefore, be used to create a reconciliation of votes cast in any individual election.
Additionally, the SURE system does not publicly display records for all types of voters, including [federal voters](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/dos/resources/voting-and-elections/directives-and-guidance/2023-UOCAVA-Federal-Voters-Guidance-2.1.pdf) and confidential voters. Confidential voters are those whose identities are required by law to be kept out of publicly available voter registration databases and lists, and they include victims of domestic violence, stalking, or human trafficking.
For these reasons, among others, FVE files exported from SURE cannot be used to reconcile vote histories from individual voter files with the number of voters who voted in a given election.
As we shared with the public in our [2020 General Election Report](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/dos/programs/voting-and-elections/2020-General-Election-Report.pdf) on our website, a total of 6,945,045 registered voters successfully cast a ballot in Pennsylvania’s Nov. 3, 2020, general election. 
As [Pennsylvania’s official certificate of ascertainment](https://www.archives.gov/files/electoral-college/2020/ascertainment-pennsylvania.pdf) – which was signed by Gov. Wolf and is electronically stored within the National Archives – shows, a total of 6,915,283 votes were cast in that year’s presidential race. The certificate breaks down the votes per presidential candidate this way:
  * Biden/Harris: 3,458,229 
  * Trump/Pence: 3,377,674 
  * Jorgensen/Cohen: 79,380 


Comparing these two official public data sources shows that 29,762 Pennsylvania voters (0.004% of the 6,945,045 voters who cast a ballot) did not have a vote recorded – for whatever reason – for one of the presidential candidates in that year’s race. This comparison clearly shows that there were not more votes than voters in Pennsylvania’s November 2020 general election.

## _1 INTRODUCTION_
This report presents data relating to the 2020 General Election, which may be helpful in mapping
* * *
additional changes to Pennsylvania’s Election Code prior to future elections. It includes some data not required by Act 35 of 2020, in order to provide helpful context for and a more complete presentation of the data.
* * *
The Department believes that the negotiations that led to the passage of Act 77 of 2019 and Act 12 of 2020 benefited all voters, and that future collaborations could do the same.
* * *
As the Department noted in the initial Act 35 of 2020 Report, and as it has repeated on multiple occasions since then, the single most important recommended change to the Election Code is the expansion of the pre-canvassing period. As we saw in the days following the 2020 General Election, even with staff working around the clock, counties were not able to finish canvassing their ballots until nearly a week after Election Day. This delay led to the dissemination of misinformation about the reliability and integrity of the election results.
* * *
An expansion of the pre-canvassing period would benefit all Pennsylvanians by facilitating more timely results and by helping to instill faith in the democratic system and the integrity of our elections. The counties overwhelmingly support this reform, and we hope the legislature shares this priority and will amend the Election Code to allow counties the flexibility to begin pre canvassing their ballots well before the close of the polls during the May 2021 Primary Election and in future elections. The data referenced and presented in this report were obtained from two sources: The Statewide Uniform Registry of Electors (SURE), which is the statewide database used by county election officials to maintain elections and voter data, and the responses to uniform surveys that the Department sent to each county board of elections. Each county board of elections is responsible for ensuring the accuracy of the data that it enters into SURE and for its own responses to the Department’s surveys. The Department does not independently verify or guarantee the accuracy of the data provided by the county boards of elections.

* * *
The number of incidents known to the Department of State, county board of elections or registration commission relating to each of the following categories: (i) An absentee ballot or mail-in ballot which was sent to the wrong individual or wrong address. (ii) An absentee ballot or mail-in ballot which was voted by an individual other than the individual who applied for the absentee ballot or mail-in ballot. (iii) An absentee ballot or mail-in ballot which was returned to the county board of elections by a means other than the elector sending the absentee ballot or mail-in ballot by mail or delivery in person. Counties collectively reported that approximately 500 ballots were sent to the wrong voter or to the wrong address. Of this total, approximately 200 were reported by Westmoreland County, 125 were reported by Lebanon County, and 105 were reported by Carbon County. Westmoreland County reported that approximately 200 ballots were sent to the wrong address due to clerical errors. Lebanon County reported that 125 ballots were sent to the wrong address due to an error during the transmission of voter data to the county’s print and mail vendor. A manual correction to a file of absentee voters resulted in the name and address information for the voters in this file being mismatched, meaning that the ballots were sent to the correct voters but at the wrong addresses. The county immediately reissued ballots for these voters as soon as it became aware of the error and thus no ballots were improperly counted. Carbon County reported that 105 voters applied for absentee and mail-in ballots and subsequently relocated due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Many of the ballot envelopes were returned to the county as undeliverable. The causes of the remaining errors and irregularities include ballots that were delivered to the wrong address due to human error or ballots that were undeliverable because voters provided an invalid address. Counties reported that 169 ballots were voted by someone other than the voter. Of this total, Philadelphia reported that 125 ballots were filled out and signed by someone other than the voter to whom the ballot was addressed. A review of issues or incidents encountered with an electronic voting system that received the
* * *
approval of the Secretary of the Commonwealth under section 1105-A of the Pennsylvania Election Code, including any technical issues encountered in polling places.
* * *
Counties reported relatively few voting system errors or issues in the 2020 General Election. Of the 13 counties that reported voting system issues, all but four (4) counties reported only isolated issues with scanners or ballot-marking devices that were quickly resolved through maintenance or replacement. For example: 1. Adams County reported a “tension issue” with its DS450 central tabulator that resulted in two ballots being pulled through the scanner at one time. This issue was resolved immediately with an adjustment to the scanner and the county was able to resume scanning ballots. 2. Armstrong County indicated that a total of 45 ballots could not be read by the central scanner and had to be tabulated manually. 3. Butler County reported that one DS850 central tabulator broke down. They were able to quickly replace it. 4. Clinton County reported that one precinct scanner malfunctioned. The scanner was replaced within 20 minutes. 5. Luzerne County reported startup errors due to human error in several of their polling places. They were able to resolve these errors with poll workers over the phone. 6. Lancaster County reported several printer connection issues at polling places due to human error. They were able to resolve these issues with poll workers over the phone. 7. McKean County reported ballot jamming issues with some precinct scanners, requiring that their precinct election officials periodically release ballot jams manually. 8. Philadelphia County reported various routine issues, such as screen calibration issues, screen freezes, and ballot jams. Each of these issues were either resolved over the phone or by technical support staff dispatched to the polling places. The county also received calls from 35-40 divisions who needed assistance over the phone or from technical support staff during the process of opening the polls 9. Snyder County reported that one precinct had difficulty closing the polls due to human error. This issue was resolved quickly with the assistance of technical support staff who were dispatched to the polling place. 3. Northumberland and McKean counties reported that they experienced ballot jams at most of their precincts at the 2020 General Election. The counties and the Department worked with ES&S; to identify the root cause of the issue. ES&S; concluded that depending on the conditions of polling place setup, static and suction can interfere with the free fall of ExpressVote ballot cards into the ballot box attached to the ExpressVote precinct scanner. To mitigate the issue ES&S; will install a conductive gasket onto the exit guide of the ExpressVote scanner to prevent static and suction. ES&S; submitted an Engineering Change Order (ECO) to the Election Assistance Commission (EAC) and the change was successfully tested by a federal voting system testing authority. Because this change is de minimis and it does not impact the accuracy or integrity of the voting system, no additional federal or state testing is necessary. The ECO was approved by the EAC on February 19, 2021, and the gaskets are being installed in Pennsylvania counties prior to the May 2021 Municipal Primary.

## CONCLUSIONS
Despite the challenges presented by COVID-19 and delayed mail service, Pennsylvanians
* * *
registered and voted in record numbers in the 2020 General Election. Approximately 9.1 million Pennsylvanians are registered to vote, 300,000 more voters than have ever previously been registered. And turnout in the November General Election included more than 6.9 million voters800,000 more Pennsylvanians than voted in any prior election in our history. The November 3,
* * *
## _2020 General Election was safe, secure, transparent, and accessible._
The Department continues to support counties beginning the pre-canvassing process as early as
* * *
three weeks before Election Day. The original Act 35 Report referenced the Democratic Primary for Auditor General as an example of why this change needs to be made. While one candidate led on the night of the primary, due to delays in canvassing of ballots, it was not until days later that a different candidate took the lead as ballots continued to be counted. The complete scope of this issue was on full display for the entire nation in the days following November 3 when this same situation played out on a much larger and more public scale. The Department agrees with the County Commissioner’s Association and others who have urged that a legislative change be made as soon as possible to allow pre-canvassing to begin earlier. Election officials at the state and local levels, Republican and Democrat alike, worked tirelessly amid a pandemic so that voters could safely and securely decide this election. Counties administered the election with the highest degree of transparency, several with a 24/7 livestream of the ballot-counting process available for anyone to watch, in addition to the bipartisan teams of election officials, party officials, and other observers of the process. The most significant change necessary to ensure even greater efficiency of our election administration is that the counties be permitted to pre-canvass mail-in and absentee ballots ahead of Election Day, like the 46 other states that permit the same.
* * *
The Department of State and the counties will continue to work together for our democracy, to ensure that the vote of every qualified voter is counted securely and accurately.
