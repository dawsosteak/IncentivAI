1. Proposed market equilibrium approach for calculating GHG, RA and RPS/SB100 avoided costs 1.1. Motivation The ACC has traditionally calculated the generation capacity, Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions, and Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) avoided costs separately. However, we are reconsidering the approach to these values to improve the alignment of the ACC with Integrated Resource Planning (IRP) modeling. IRP modeling chooses capacity expansions based on the need to meet demand (i.e., generation capacity for Resource Adequacy purposes), to meet California’s GHG goals, and to meet Renewable Portfolio Standard/SB 100 requirements. There is an inter-relationship between these three objectives, such that the annual cost of new generation capacity can be partially attributed to each one. The goal of this task is to develop an updated approach to calculating GHG, Resource Adequacy (RA), and RPS / SB 100 avoided costs that: a. better aligns the IDER ACC proceeding with IRP (RESOLVE + SERVM) modeling, b. creates a clearly justifiable, technology-agnostic approach to avoided costs, and c. addresses stakeholder comments in previous cycles about potential double-counting of value. In 2019, Staff proposed aligning the ACC with IRP by using IRP modeling outputs as avoided cost inputs – a proposal which was adopted and implemented with the 2020 ACC. At that time, we envisioned that the annual capacity price from IRP modeling could be used to determine the avoided cost of generation capacity. However, that price turned out to be quite volatile, and heavily influenced by the availability of the federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC). According to IRP’s RESOLVE model, the availability of the ITC results in capacity build-out occurring earlier than needed. While this minimizes costs for ratepayers, it is not a good reflection of the long-term value of a DER that would avoid that capacity. Hence, the 2020 ACC instead continued to use the long-standing method of basing the avoided cost of generation capacity (i.e., RA value) on the cost of building new generation. However, the 2020 ACC switched from using a Combustion Turbine to using a 4-hour Storage Battery as the marginal capacity unit, consistent with IRP modeling. In addition, the avoided cost of RPS was eliminated from the ACC because it was determined that the need to meet the RPS was not a binding constraint in the IRP model. While this was a reasonable stopgap approach, we believe that the relationship between avoided RPS, RA, and GHG costs are more complicated than we previously thought. IRP modeling indicates that the annual cost of capacity expansion is due to all three needs – the need to meet RA requirements (i.e., to meet demand and provide reliability), to meet GHG goals, and to meet the RPS/SB 100 goals. We propose to develop a technology-agnostic market equilibrium approach to jointly model these three avoided costs. Our approach is based on the premise that the GHG, RA, and RPS/SB100 prices should be determined jointly rather than independently and should be calculated based on the portfolio of resources selected in the IRP rather than a single marginal resource. This approach would be consistent with the IRP RESOLVE portfolio modeling, which solves for a portfolio of resources that simultaneously meets reliability, GHG and renewable portfolio standard (RPS) and SB 100 clean energy targets. Contrast this with the current ACC approach, which uses: • 2030 GHG shadow price from IRP RESOLVE modeling as the basis for the avoided cost of GHG • 4-hour utility scale lithium-ion energy storage plant as the basis for generation capacity 3

Non-hourly Avoided Costs The avoided cost categories currently included in the ACC are quantified on an hourly basis or are coincident peak load driven costs allocated to peak load hours. Stakeholders have suggested inclusion of additional avoided cost categories that are not driven by hourly or coincident peak loads. In this section we consider whether and how such avoided costs can be included in the ACC. The purpose of the ACC is to provide consistent, technology-agnostic avoided costs that are broadly applicable to DERs. The secondary distribution costs described below are a non-hourly avoided cost that would apply to all DER. The project-based natural gas infrastructure and refrigerant avoided costs are non-hourly avoided costs that are specific to a subset of DERs. These benefits are described here to promote consistency in DER cost-benefit analysis, but are not applicable to all DERs. We note that benefits or avoided costs not included in the ACC may nevertheless be considered in other proceedings evaluating specific DERs or programs as appropriate. 3.1. Secondary Distribution Distribution avoided costs are allocated to distribution peak load hours based on the presumption that they are driven by aggregate coincident peak loads, which is true for primary distribution marginal costs. Secondary distribution costs are not driven by the diversified aggregate peak demand of hundreds or thousands of customers, but are instead driven by individual or small groups of customers. As such the timing of the peaks on secondary equipment are far less predictable than equipment further upstream on the electrical grid, and the diversity benefits from heterogeneous customers is largely non-existent. PG&E; is proposing to change the way secondary capacity costs are allocated in their ongoing GRC 2020 Phase II (A.19-11-019). In the ongoing proceeding, PG&E; is proposing to use the non-coincident final line transformer (FLT) demand to allocate secondary distribution costs (the “distribution FLT” method). To be clear, PG&E; is not proposing the elimination of the secondary capacity costs in the ongoing GRC Phase II, but rather just proposing a different allocation method for those costs. The ACC currently has a method for allocating primary distribution costs to aggregate coincident peak load hours, but does not have corresponding approach for allocating secondary costs based on the PG&E; distribution FLT or other method. The secondary distribution costs described below are a non-hourly avoided cost that would apply to all DER. The project-based natural gas infrastructure and refrigerant avoided costs are non-hourly avoided costs that are specific to a subset of DERs. These benefits are described here to promote consistency in DER cost-benefit analysis, but are not applicable to all DERs. We note that benefits or avoided costs not included in the ACC may nevertheless be considered in other proceedings evaluating specific DERs or programs as appropriate. 3.1. Secondary Distribution Distribution avoided costs are allocated to distribution peak load hours based on the presumption that they are driven by aggregate coincident peak loads, which is true for primary distribution marginal costs. Secondary distribution costs are not driven by the diversified aggregate peak demand of hundreds or thousands of customers, but are instead driven by individual or small groups of customers. As such the timing of the peaks on secondary equipment are far less predictable than equipment further upstream on the electrical grid, and the diversity benefits from heterogeneous customers is largely non-existent. PG&E; is proposing to change the way secondary capacity costs are allocated in their ongoing GRC 2020 Phase II (A.19-11-019). In the ongoing proceeding, PG&E; is proposing to use the non-coincident final line transformer (FLT) demand to allocate secondary distribution costs (the “distribution FLT” method). To be clear, PG&E; is not proposing the elimination of the secondary capacity costs in the ongoing GRC Phase II, but rather just proposing a different allocation method for those costs. The ACC currently has a method for allocating primary distribution costs to aggregate coincident peak load hours, but does not have corresponding approach for allocating secondary costs based on the PG&E; distribution FLT or other method. 6. Natural Gas Avoided Costs 6.1. Gas Price Forecasts The CEC IEPR provides the natural gas price forecast that is used in the in the IRP proceeding. The electric and gas ACCs, on the other hand, both use several years of forward natural gas prices from the ICE futures exchange in the short term, and the IEPR forecast in the long term. The use of forward market prices for natural gas price forecasts was first introduced in the Market Price Referent (MPR) used in the early years of the RPS proceeding. Market prices were an important consideration in comparing the cost of competitively procured renewable and fossil generation. In the current policy environment focused on clean energy, market based natural gas prices no longer have the significant impact on market prices and procurement that they once did. In addition, using different gas price forecasts for the ACC and IRP requires additional processing of RESOLVE and SERVM. This adds unnecessary complexity and opportunity for error in producing several years of hourly energy prices. Staff Proposal Recommendation We propose to use the IEPR gas price forecast, which is currently used in the IRP, for short and long term natural gas prices in the electric and gas ACCs, to promote consistency and reduce unnecessary complexity. 6.2. Natural Gas GHG Adder For the electric sector, the ACC currently derives the avoided cost of GHG from IRP RESOLVE modeling of the electric sector resource portfolio required to meet electric sector GHG targets. The ACC currently uses this same electric sector GHG value for avoided GHG emissions from the natural gas sector. To date, there has been no study or proceeding that has developed an avoided cost of GHG emissions separately for the natural gas sector based on the cost of renewable natural gas (RNG) supply options. The challenges of decarbonizing the natural gas sector are well enumerated in other studies and will not be repeated here (California Energy Commission, 2020) (California Energy Commission, 2021) (Energy and Environmental Economics, 2020). There are three central challenges to developing an avoided cost of GHG emissions specific to the natural gas sector: • The CPUC IRP proceeding has set specific emission targets for the electric sector, but no proceeding has yet set a similar natural gas sector specific emissions target. • The supply of lower cost RNG options is limited and the options needed to meet forecast demand are both extremely high cost and not technically developed at scale. A forecast of marginal RNG supply cost will therefore be both very high and very uncertain. • The building, transportation and industrial sectors will all be competing for limited technically feasible and low cost RNG supplies, requiring an economy wide view beyond just the natural gas sector to set targets and model costs. Scarcity Pricing Approach The intent of scarcity pricing adjustment is to capture systematic differences within the forecasted prices which fail to capture scarcity in real historical energy prices and add that into the forecast. Like all models, SERVM is a simplified and idealized representation of a real system and as such, will naturally fail to reflect real-world incidents that may trigger scarcity (for example, line outages or congestion). Nor will it capture scarcity-based behavior exhibited by participants in actual markets. For the 2022 ACC, we propose to deploy a similar approach as in 2021 ACC. We anticipate for one set of scarcity factors to be developed and applied to all price regions modeled; this is based on the understanding that scarcity conditions in CA’s major demand center tends to ripple across all zones within the state. The scarcity adjustments across zones in the state should therefore be coordinated. For the selected scarcity-setting zone, E3 will benchmark SERVM prices before scarcity adjustments against actual 2020 historical prices. We plan to back out the marginal heat rates as implied by prices in the benchmark year rather than work directly with prices. Working with implied heat rates effectively factors out the differences in gas and carbon prices, which are temporally variable and does not offer specific inside into the electricity system. In this benchmarking step, we will look to identify the number of hours in which scarcity exists in historical benchmark year but is not reflected by the forecasted prices. These hours will then be scaled up to approximate the historical pattern and magnitude. Finally, it is assumed that these systematic modeling shortcomings between SERVM forecast and the real world persists across all years modeled. This calculated set of scarcity scalars based on this benchmarked year will be applied to each year of the SERVM forecast to adjust for scarcity. There is no proposal to change the calendar year used in the 2022 ACC. It will continue to use the calendar year of 2020. This section is intended to clarify the time zone and calendar year of avoided costs in the ACC. Avoided costs in the ACC are in Pacific Standard Time and aligned with the calendar year shown in the timestamp. The timestamp of ACC goes from January 1 of 2020 to December 30 of 2020 without adjustment for daylight savings and without the last day of the leap year to ensure a continuous stream of avoided costs. This is consistent with the SERVM dispatch output data in Pacific Standard Time that contains continuous 8760 hours of prices for each study year with the last day of leap year cut off. When transforming SERVM raw prices into ACC inputs, Energy Division Consultant E3 shifts prices from SERVM in each year to match the day of week of the 2020 year. For example, since January 1 2020 is a Wednesday, 2021 SERVM prices are shifted upward such that the first day of the 2021 avoided costs also starts on Wednesday. After aligning prices of each year to 2020 calendar year, prices of holidays in each year are replaced with the respective holiday prices of the respective year. 9. The resource portfolio modeled in RESOLVE and in SERVM are developed to meet a maximum total GHG emissions for the electric sector (e.g., 30 million metric tons), given the retail electric load forecast by the CEC IEPR. The constraint on the portfolio is the total allowable emissions to serve the retail load forecast and the average grid emissions intensity will decline over time to meet stricter GHG emission targets. SERVM modeling of that portfolio will also provide marginal hourly emissions. The marginal hourly emissions will also decline over time, but will tend to be higher than the average grid emissions as dispatchable fossil units will often be the marginal units kept online to provide operating reserves. The first step in the calculation of GHG avoided costs is based on the marginal emissions as calculated by SERVM. The marginal emissions impact of adding or decreasing load provides only a partial picture. We must also consider how both the portfolio and the allowable GHG emissions target would adjust when load is added or removed on the margin. The clearest example is made by considering building and transportation electrification. These measures reduce GHG emissions overall, but add load to the electric system. If electrification load were added to an electric sector IRP portfolio, one would expect the allowable GHG emissions from the electric sector to increase proportionally, not to remain fixed at the original total emissions target. The ACC is a simplified, static snapshot of the marginal costs for a given electric sector resource portfolio and a given GHG emissions target. We need a correspondingly simple and straightforward approach to reflect a proportional reallocation of allowable GHG emissions between the transportation, building and electric sectors with increased electrification load. The approach used in the ACC is to use the average grid emissions intensity for the modeled IRP portfolio to calculate a Step 2 portfolio rebalancing impact. The simplifying assumption is to assume the average grid intensity is a reasonable reflection of the electric sectors proportional responsibility for meeting California’s total GHG emissions target. Thus, when considering incremental load growth from electrification, the allowable GHG emissions from the electric sector increases proportionally, and the allowable increase is the incremental load in kWh times the average grid emissions intensity in GHG/kWh.

Additional refrigerant values We propose to update the list of refrigerants to reflect the most current set of refrigerants available from the CARB database. In addition to the refrigerants available in the database, an optional input will be added to allow users to specify additional refrigerants and their GWPs to be used in the calculator. Increased Supply Costs The ACC was originally created to estimate the benefits, in the form of avoided costs, of DERs. It is still used primarily for that purpose. However, because of the wealth of data contained in the ACC, it has also been used for other purposes, such as estimating GHG emissions, creating incentives and tariffs, and calculating the increased supply costs that result from fuel substitution and fuel switching. The ACC is currently used to determine the increased supply costs of the fuel substitution measures that are part of the energy efficiency portfolio, and will likely be needed to determine these costs for future cost effectiveness evaluation of our emerging building and transportation decarbonization programs. It is logical to use marginal avoided costs from the ACC to determine the increased supply costs of electricity that results from decarbonization efforts, but it has never been specifically adopted in a CPUC proceeding or subject to stakeholder review. We do not propose to change this practice, but we do have several questions for stakeholder input. Staff Proposal Questions We seek party comments on whether the ACC should be used to calculate increased supply costs of decarbonization efforts. Specific issues parties should address in their comments include: • Are the marginal avoided costs determined in the ACC, based on the IRP No New DER scenario, the appropriate basis for increased supply costs? • Are there any modifications to the current method, or any new methods, that would make this calculation more accurate? 38
