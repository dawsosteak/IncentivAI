# PA Kids' Corner
Whether you're visiting Pennsylvania for the first time, or just getting to know more about the state you live in, here are all the fun facts and must-know details about our commonwealth. 
Did you know Pennsylvania is technically a commonwealth and not a state? Keep reading to learn more. 
## Commonwealth 
“Commonwealth” is an Old English term for a political community founded for the common good. There’s no difference between a Commonwealth and a state in how the government is run but the name remains because Pennsylvania is a community providing for the good of everyone! 
![Commonwealth state outline](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/commonwealth-outline?ts=1758720116119&dpr=off)
* * *
## Keystone State
A keystone is a wedge-shaped stone found at the top of an arch. The stone holds all the other pieces in place – without it the arch would collapse! In early America, Pennsylvania was the center state of the 13 colonies. Pennsylvania also played a vital role in holding together the states of the newly formed union. In the 1800s, the “Keystone State” title stuck! 
![Keystone icon](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/keystone?ts=1758720123787&dpr=off)
* * *
## State Coat of Arms
The coat of arms, which is displayed on Pennsylvania’s flag, was based on a 1778 design by Caleb Lownes. The coat of arms displays a shield surrounded by horses and a bald eagle. The shield shows a ship carrying Pennsylvania exports around the world, a clay pillow to symbolize our natural resources, and three sheaves of wheat representing our fields, as well as human action. Below the shield there is an olive branch and a cornstalk, representing peace and prosperity. The **state motto** , “Virtue Liberty and Independence” appears on the very bottom of the coat of arms. 
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/coat-0f-arms?ts=1758720098858&dpr=off)
* * *
## State Capitol - Harrisburg
Set in south-central Pennsylvania, the capitol of the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania is Harrisburg. The city of Harrisburg sits along the Susquehanna River and has a population of a little over 50,000 people, making it the ninth largest city in the Commonwealth. Before Europeans came to what is now Pennsylvania, the Susquehannocks and Lenape (or Delaware) tribes lived where modern-day Harrisburg sits, along with the Shawnee and Nanticoke tribes. 
![Photograph of the city of Harrisburg](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/harrisburg-edited?ts=1758720106583&dpr=off)
* * *
## How did PA get its name?
Pennsylvania was first and originally inhabited by the indigenous people from the Susquehannock, Lenape (or Delaware), Shawnee, and Nanticoke tribes. In 1681 the area was colonized and became an English colony when William Penn came to the area for religious freedom. The king at the time who ‘owned’ the colonized land, gave the ‘new’ English colony, to William Penn and named the area Pennsylvania which in Latin translates to Penn’s Woods. 
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/will-penn-2?ts=1758720089331&dpr=off)
* * *
## PA Size & Population
Pennsylvania is a much larger state than most people realize. Pennsylvania is 309 miles long and 174 miles wide, over 46,000 square miles. The Commonwealth is nearly 38 times larger than Rhode Island and ranks 33rd overall. Nearly 13 million people from all different backgrounds and walks of life reside in this large and diverse state. 
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/population?ts=1758720079562&dpr=off)
## State Symbols
##  State Amphibian - Eastern Hellbender
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/hellbender-edited?ts=1752701225035&dpr=off)
Snot otter, mud devil, devil dog, and even lasagna lizard. These are all nicknames for the Pennsylvania state amphibian – the Eastern Hellbender. The Hellbender is a nocturnal aquatic species found in Pennsylvania streams and rivers with larger rocks. They are a large flat salamander that eat small fish, crayfish, and hellgrammites.
The Hellbender is a hardy salamander that can live up to fifty years in the proper environment and are often used as an indicator of the health of a waterway since they need clean and almost perfect water conditions to survive.
In 2019, the Eastern Hellbender was designated as the state amphibian.
[ Color your own Eastern Hellbender and learn more about the state amphibian! (opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/fishandboat/documents/education/activitiesandeducationportal/coloreasthellbndr.pdf)
##  State Animal - White-Tailed Deer
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/deer?ts=1752701222012&dpr=off)
Early Pennsylvanians and Native Americans relied on the white-tailed deer for necessities such as food, clothing, and shelter. Today, these animals continue to flourish in Pennsylvania’s forests, with an estimated 1.5 million deer in the state. 
The white-tailed deer was designated as the state animal on October 2nd, 1959.
[ Want to know more about our state animal? Submit a question to the deer experts. ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/pgc/wildlife/discover-pa-wildlife/white-tailed-deer/ask-a-deer-question)
##  State Beverage - Milk
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/cow-edited?ts=1752701232748&dpr=off)
MOOOO! Believe it or not, the state beverage is cows milk. Pennsylvania has a lot of farmland throughout the Commonwealth and ranks 7th in the nation for milk production! The dairy cows in Pennsylvania produce almost 10 billion pound of milk annually! With all the farmers and milk produced it is great for the Commonwealth’s state economy. So when you see cows out in the pasture next time, remember that they are producing our state beverage!
Milk was designated as the state beverage on April 29th, 1982. 
[ Did you know you can use milk to make your own butter? ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/farmshow/pa-farm-show/participate-virtually/agexplorers/dairy)
##  State Bird - Ruffed Grouse
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/ruffed-grouse-edited?ts=1752701215004&dpr=off)
Designated the state bird on June 22, 1931, the Ruffed Grouse is a familiar sight in the many forests of Pennsylvania. Also known as a partridge, the Ruffed Grouse was a vital source of food supply for early settlers in Pennsylvania. 
[ Learn how to help Pennsylvania birds by building your own bird feeder. (opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/pgc/documents/education/foreducators/documents/how%20to%20make%20a%20bird%20feeder.pdf)
##  State Dog - Great Dane
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/great-dane-1?ts=1752701221912&dpr=off)
A giant breed, described as strong, intelligent, powerful, and faithful, the Great Dane became the official state dog of Pennsylvania in 1965. William Penn, the founder of Pennsylvania, even had a Great Dane! A portrait of him and his Great Dane hangs in the Governor’s Reception Room in the Pennsylvania Capitol. 
[ Find out how the Commonwealth helps keep dogs safe. ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/farmshow/pa-farm-show/participate-virtually/agexplorers/dog-licensing)
##  State Fish - Brook Trout
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/fish-edited?ts=1752701220872&dpr=off)
Found in the cold clear waters of Pennsylvania’s streams and ponds is the state fish, the native Brook Trout. The Brook Trout is a part of the char group of the salmon family and can live upwards of four years. The Brook Trout eats insects both above and below the water!
The PA Department of Conservation and Natural Resources is making sure the Brook Trout’s habitat remains clean with deep water projects and more forest buffer plantings to improve water quality and cooler stream temperatures.
The Brook Trout was designated as the state fish on March 9th, 1970. 
[ What makes Brook Trout so special? See how they stand out in a school! (opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/fishandboat/documents/education/activitiesandeducationportal/troutid.pdf)
##  State Flower - Mountain Laurel
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/flower-edited?ts=1752701224274&dpr=off)
The Mountain Laurel is a common sight throughout Pennsylvania when the flower blooms in the summer. Designated on May 5, 1933 by Governor Gifford Pinchot, the flower was a favorite of the Pennsylvania House and Senate to become the state flower.
[ Find our state flower where it blooms the brightest — Laurel Mountain State Park. ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/dcnr/recreation/where-to-go/state-parks/find-a-park/laurel-mountain-state-park)
##  State Fossil - Eldredgeops rana (Trilobite)
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/fossill-edited-scaled?ts=1752701224855&dpr=off)
Looking like the modern-day pill bug, the Eldredgeops rana, also known as the Trilobite, is the Pennsylvania state fossil! The prehistoric invertebrate lived in the Commonwealth more than 250 million years ago. The trilobite is related to the modern-day crab, lobster, shrimp, some insects, and even spiders.
The feature of the Eldredgeops rana that sets it apart from other trilobites is the large frog like eyes. Be sure to keep your eyes open for this fossil next time your rock hunting!
Eldredgeops rana was designated as the state fossil on December 5th, 1988. 
[ Uncover the secrets of ancient life hidden in fossils! ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/dcnr/education/geology-education/identifying-and-collecting)
##  State Insect - Firefly
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/firefly-edited?ts=1752701222832&dpr=off)
Fireflies, the state insect of Pennsylvania, are not actually flies… they are beetles! Also known as lightning bugs, they can be seen lighting up the Pennsylvania skies in the summer evenings with their soft yellow glow.
The firefly’s favorite meals include slugs, earthworms, and even snails. The firefly’s numbers are declining from habitat loss, pesticide use, and light pollution. We can help the firefly population by turning our lights off at night and planting native Pennsylvania flowers and vegetation at our homes!
The firefly was designated as the state insect on April 10th, 1974. 
[ Celebrate Native Species Day and shine a light on our glowing native insect! ](https://www.pa.gov/agencies/pda/about-pda/boards-commissions/governors-invasive-species-council/pennsylvania-native-species-day)
##  State Tree - Eastern Hemlock
![](https://s7d9.scene7.com/is/image/statepa/tree-edited?ts=1752701228139&dpr=off)
Commonly found throughout Pennsylvania’s forests, the Eastern Hemlock became the official state tree in 1931. Proving to be incredibly useful for early settlers, the Eastern Hemlock provided sturdy wood for log cabins, as well as tannic acid for producing leather. 
[ Learn how you can protect our state tree from the spotted lanternfly. (opens in a new tab)](https://www.pa.gov/content/dam/copapwp-pagov/en/pda/documents/plants_land_water/plantindustry/entomology/spotted_lanternfly/resources/documents/SLF%20Control%20Storybook.pdf)

## PA’s Amphibian and Reptile Coloring Series
**Eastern Hellbender**
All About Me: I am a Hellbender, a member of the Giant Salamander family. We are Pennsylvania’s official state amphibian! We can grow from 11 to 20 inches long. People long ago believed we had a venomous bite, but that’s not true. We are completely aquatic and nocturnal. We are usually gray in color, but some of us are brown or even black. Our belly is light in color. Sometimes we have black spots on our backs and sides. Our bodies and heads are flattened. Several flaps of wrinkled skin hang along our lower sides. We have tiny eyes. We start out with external gills that we lose when we reach four or five inches in length. You may see gill openings on adults. Our legs are short and stout. Our tails are flat and rudderlike to help us swim. We are found in the Susquehanna and Ohio River watersheds; but we don’t live in the Delaware River watershed. We live in fast-moving, mid-sized streams and the channels of clear rivers. We prefer habitat with plenty of boulders, large stones, snags and other large, loose debris. We take refuge beneath this cover by day, coming out to forage for food at night. We eat crayfish, snails, aquatic insects, and worms. www.fishandboat.com

Kenadie G., Third Grade, Levittown PA October 2019
* * *

Tin Can Bird Feeder
* * *
What will you need? Tin can, acrylic or multi-surface paint, paint brushes, cup with water, paper towels, newspaper or disposable tablecloth, sticks or twigs, string or ribbon, craft glue, scissors, bird seed
* * *
Step By Step Instructions 1. Find an empty soup or vegetable can. Make sure the can is clean and remove the label.
* * *
2. Once your can is clean and dry, take a paint brush and paint the outside of your can.
* * *
3. Go outside with you parents’ permission and find a stick you can glue to your can as a perch or place for a bird to sit. A good stick will be about the width of your pointer finger and the length of the can.
* * *
4. Glue the stick on the outside of the can, so that a couple inches of the stick extends past the can opening.
* * *
5. Tie two equally sized strings or ribbons around the outside of the can, one towards the open end and one towards the closed end. These strings/ribbons will be used to tie your bird feeder to a tree limb.
* * *
6. Fill your bird feeder with bird seed. And hang horizontally from a tree limb in your backyard.
