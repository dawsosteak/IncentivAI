Order Instituting Rulemaking to Create a Consistent Regulatory Framework for the Guidance, Planning and Evaluation of Integrated Distributed Energy Resources.
* * *
Rulemaking 14-10-003

ORDER DENYING REHEARING OF DECISION (D.) 19-03-023
* * *
I. INTRODUCTION Today’s decision disposes of the application for rehearing of Decision (D.) 19-03-023 (or “Decision”), filed by the Clean Coalition (“CC” or “Clean Coalition”). On October 2, 2014, the Commission opened Rulemaking (R.)14-08-013 to create a consistent regulatory framework for the guidance, planning, and evaluation of integrated distribution energy resources. CC participated in the proceeding by filing documents and comments. On February 16, 2017, CC filed an intervenor compensation claim for contributions to D.16-12-036 and D.15-09-022. D.19-03-023 denied all of CC’s claims. The Decision found CC ineligible for intervenor compensation because it is not an organization representing the interests of residential customers, as required by Public Utilities Code section 1802(b)(1)(C). CC timely filed an Application for Rehearing of D.19-03-023. No party filed a response. CC alleges the Decision erred in the following ways: (1) it relied on a series of conclusions and statements that were not based in the record; (2) it cited evidence not properly in the record; (3) the assigned Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) violated due process by acting as investigator, prosecutor and judge and unfairly denied CC the ability to rebut the allegations; (4) certain facts in the record contradicted the findings and certain other findings are not supported; (5) the Decision introduced a new
* * *
R.14-10-003 L/ice 376057857 2 and ill-defined standard regarding intervenors’ eligibility for compensation; and (6) it penalized CC for the Commission’s failure to render preliminary decisions on the NOI. As relief, it seeks a rehearing of D.19-03-023. We have carefully reviewed each and every allegation in CC’s rehearing application. We find that the evidence in the record supports our conclusion in D.19-03-023 that CC relationships and projects with other entities meant that it was not single-mindedly devoted to representing the interests of underrepresented residential customers in the underlying proceeding (R.14-10-003), and therefore, not compensable. Therefore, rehearing of D.19-03-023 is denied as no legal error has been demonstrated. II. DISCUSSION The intervenor compensation program encourages the “effective and efficient participation of all groups that have a stake in the public utility regulation process.” (Pub. Util. Code § 1801.3(b).) It awards the costs of an individual’s participation if certain criteria are met. The statute permits three categories of customers to apply for an award: (1) actual utility customers, (2) authorized representatives of utility customers, and (3) organizations whose articles and bylaws authorize the representation of residential and the small commercial customers. (Pub. Util. Code § 1802(b)(1).) Clean Coalition has applied for intervenor compensation under the third category. To be eligible for compensation under this third category, the organization must be focused
* * *
> “single-mindedly” on pursuing the interests of utility customers that it purports to
represent in the proceeding. In addition to this foundational requirement, the applicant must also demonstrate significant financial hardship. (Pub. Util. Code § 1802(h).) Clean Coalition has frequently participated in Commission proceedings for over a decade and, until recently, had been awarded intervenor compensation for some of those efforts. In 2015, CC filed some information in a proceeding that prompted the Commission to reevaluate its eligibility for intervenor compensation. This new information indicated that CC was focused on serving entities in the renewable industry and markets. This led the Commission to find that Clean Coalition was no longer an organization dedicated to representing the interests of small commercial and residential
* * *

(ALJ Ruling Rejecting Clean Coalition’s Amended Notice of Intent to Claim Intervenor Compensation [A.15-02-009] 6/30/16 at pp. 6-9.) In D.18-11-010, the Commission conducted a comprehensive review of CC’s filings and activities to evaluate its eligibility for intervenor compensation. The Commission concluded that CC did not demonstrate that it solely represented the interests of residential customers and was therefore ineligible for intervenor compensation. (D.18-11-010 at p. 20.) That decision also found that CC could not demonstrate significant financial hardship because it represented entities that had an economic interest in the outcome of the proceedings and that those entities did not have a cost barrier to participation. (D.18-11-010 at pp. 21-22.) CC filed an application for rehearing of D.18-11-010. The Commission denied its request for rehearing in D.20-09-040. This decision relies on the same reasoning of D.18-11-010 in denying the application for intervenor compensation. CC’s application for rehearing, in turn, raised nearly identical arguments to those in its prior rehearing request for D.18-11-010. This application for rehearing did not introduce any new evidentiary arguments or legal issues. And the evidence in the record continues to support the Commission’s conclusion that CC was not eligible for intervenor compensation because it does not exclusively represent underrepresented ratepayers. Thus, CC has not demonstrated that the Commission committed legal error based on the law or facts of this case. Therefore, for the reasons explained in D.20-09-040 (the recent decision on rehearing of D.18-11-010), this application for rehearing is also denied. III. CONCLUSION Based on our review of CC’s rehearing application, we find the evidence in the record supports our conclusion that Clean Coalition is not eligible for intervenor compensation. We find there is no merit to CC’s claims of legal error in the Decision. Accordingly, rehearing of D.19-03-023 is denied.
