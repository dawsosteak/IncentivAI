Brief description of Decision: D.21-02-006 adopted two pilot frameworks – the Partnership Pilot and the Standard-Offer-Contract Pilot – as mechanisms to promote utility procurement of distributed energy resources (DER) as an alternative to utility grid investments, and as a more streamlined tariff-like mechanism to procure DER services. The Partnership Pilot promotes procurement of aggregated behind-the-meter (BTM) resources, while the SOC promotes procurement of in-front-of-meter (OFM) wholesale resources targeted in the DIDF process.
* * *
Resolution E-5150 adopted a number of corrections and minor changes to the Avoided Cost Calculator for 2021 and resulted in a lower and more reasonable forecast of avoided costs for DERs in future years.

C. Additional Comments on Part II: # Intervenor’s Comment CPUC Discussion
* * *
## _1 Partial Success:_
The Commission should compensate TURN for all work in this proceeding, despite
* * *
the fact that the Commission did not adopt some of TURN’s policy recommendations.
* * *
Such an outcome is consistent with the statutory definition of “substantial contribution” in Section 1802 of the PU Code, which states that a contribution results because the Commission “has adopted in whole or in part one or more factual contentions, legal contentions, or specific policy or procedural recommendations presented by the customer.” The standard for an award of intervenor compensation is whether TURN made a substantial contribution to the Commission’s decision, not whether TURN prevailed on all issues.
* * *
The Commission has interpreted the “in whole or in part” provision, in conjunction with Section 1801.3, so as to effectuate the legislature’s intent to encourage effective and efficient intervenor participation. The Commission has established as a general proposition that when a party makes a substantial contribution in a multi-issue proceeding, it is entitled to compensation for time and expenses even if it does not prevail on some of the issues. See, for example, D.98-04-028 (awarding TURN full compensation in CTC proceeding, even though TURN did not prevail on all issues); D.98-08-016, pp. 6, 12 (awarding TURN full compensation in SoCalGas PBR proceeding); D.00-02-008, pp. 4-7, 10 (awarding TURN full compensation even though we unsuccessfully opposed settlement). Noted

R.14-10-003 ALJ/KHY/mef7 PART III: REASONABLENESS OF REQUESTED COMPENSATION A. General Claim of Reasonableness (§ 1801 and § 1806): a. Intervenor’s claim of cost reasonableness: This proceeding addressed significant policy issues concerning procurement of distributed energy resources and the valuation of such resources using the Avoided Cost Calculator. Since there were no specific spending proposals addressed in this phase of the proceeding, TURN’s participation could not reasonably lead to a specific reduction in utility revenue requirements. TURN’s participation should result in ratepayer benefits in the coming years due to more accurate evaluation of supply- and demand-side resources and programs through the use of the ACC. TURN’s participation also helped develop a proposed for procurement that does not overpay for DERs or shift risks of non-performance entirely onto ratepayers, thus providing future financial benefits to ratepayers, and hopefully promoting the use of DERs instead of alternative utility investments. CPUC Discussion Noted b. Reasonableness of hours claimed: Summary: TURN devoted a total of 62 hours of attorney and expert time (excluding work on the compensation request) on work related to the decision and resolution, including 50 hours of attorney time and 12 hours of expert witness time. TURN submits that the Commission should find the hours requested here to be reasonable under the circumstances, and that TURN’s showing supports that conclusion. However, should the Commission believe that more information is needed or that a different approach to discussing the reasonableness of the requested hours is warranted here, TURN requests the opportunity to supplement this section of the request.
