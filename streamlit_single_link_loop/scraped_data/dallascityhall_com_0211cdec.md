Who is governed by the duties of 12A?
The Code of Ethics applies to city employees and officials (current and after leaving the city for a period of time), and people doing business with the city including lobbyist.
Do I have a duty to report a violation?
Yes. If you know a violation has occurred. You may report a violation if you think there might be a violation.
Am I required to cooperate with an investigation?
Yes. It is a violation of the Code of Ethics if you do not.
What happens if I lie to OIG investigators?
Lying to an OIG investigator is a form of interference and is therefore a violation of the Code of Ethics.
Is it a violation if I get someone else to violate the Code?
Yes
Am I protected from whistleblower retaliation under the Code or other laws?
Yes. Both Code of Ethics and the Whistleblower Act prohibit retaliation.
Is it okay to discuss ongoing investigations?
No. Investigations are confidential until made public by the inspector general.
What happens if I interfere with an investigation?
You are subject to the sanctions and discipline outlined in the Code of Ethics.
Is there a time limit in which a complaint must be made?
Yes. The Ethics Advisory Commission may not consider any alleged or suspected violation that occurred more than one year before the date of the filing of a complaint or more than one year before the initiation of an investigation by the inspector general. This can be tolled during any time an investigation is halted due to ongoing criminal or civil litigation.
Who can file a complaint?
Any person may file a complaint with the inspector general if the complainant, either at the time the alleged violation of this chapter occurred or at the time the complaint is submitted, is a resident for the city, a person doing business or attempting to do business with the city, or a city official or employee, and the complainant believes there has been a violation of this chapter.
Who manages the hotline and who will see it?
The hotline is managed by a third party outside of the city. The complaint will be seen by the inspector general and the OIG team once it has been made through the Whistle.
What makes a complaint viable?
A complaint becomes viable for a hearing if the OIG can prove that a violation has occurred by the burden of proof known as “preponderance of the evidence” for administrative cases. For criminal cases, the burden of proof is “beyond a reasonable doubt.” Corruption and fraud cases are criminal and require special tools and authority to investigate. A bill has been filed with the State Legislature seeking law enforcement authority to empower the OIG with the necessary tools to complete investigations in criminal cases.
What are violations that the OIG investigates?
The OIG has jurisdiction to investigate cases of fraud, waste, abuse, corruption, and ethics violations. Other matters are referred out, including criminal cases. Corruption and fraud cases are criminal and require special tools and authority to investigate. A bill has been filed with the State Legislature seeking law enforcement authority to empower the OIG with the necessary tools to complete investigations in criminal cases.
What are violations that the OIG does not generally investigate?
Here is a summary of many provisions in the Code dealing with conflicts of interest that apply to city employees, officials, and persons doing business with the city. Note, this summary is NOT a substitute for the Code – it is only meant to orient you to the contents of the Code. There are rules that only apply to city officials and other rules that only apply to employees. Violations include:
  1. Gifts: You cannot accept a gift if it looks like it that would reasonably tend to influence or reward official conduct, or that is intended to influence or reward official conduct. You cannot take any form of cash from a person or business who is or wants to do business with the city, and you cannot accept anything but a plaque in return for doing your job. Similarly, a person or business must not offer gifts that would reasonably tend to influence or reward official conduct, or that are intended to influence or reward official conduct. Note, there are reporting requirements to consider if you receive a gift.
  2. Personal benefit to others: You cannot use your position to do or avoid doing your job if that results in a personal benefit for a relative, business, financial, or employment relationship. “Relative” includes a current or former spouse, children, siblings, parents, and anyone listed as dependent on your latest individual federal income tax return.
  3. Substantial interest: Similarly, we must recuse ourselves before a vote or decision on any matter involving a business entity or real property where we, or our relative, hold a substantial interest if: the action on the business entity will have a special economic effect that is distinguishable from the effect on the public; or it is reasonably foreseeable that an action on the matter will have a specific economic effect on the value of the property that is distinguishable from the effect on the public. Substantial interest in a business is defined as owning 10 percent or more of the voting stock or shares, or owning either 10 percent or more or $15,000 or more of the fair market value of the business entity; or funds received exceed 10 percent of the person’s gross income for the previous year. Substantial interest in real property is defined as equitable or legal ownership with a fair market value of $2,500 or more.
  4. Miscellaneous conflicts: You cannot acquire an interest in impending matters or decided matters of the city. Also, you cannot arrange for or receive a benefit from any other person. Finally, you cannot award or participate in a matter that benefits a former employer within twelve months of starting your position with the city.
  5. Donations: Like gifts, you cannot accept a donation if it looks like it would reasonably tend to influence or reward official conduct, or that are intended to influence or reward official conduct. Note, there are reports and other requirements to consider if you receive a donation.
  6. Nepotism: You cannot influence the appointment or hiring of a relative, and you cannot be the immediate supervisor of a relative.
  7. Confidential Information: You cannot use your position to access confidential government information to secure official information for any reason but doing your job. Similarly, you cannot disclose official information, including disclosure of a closed meeting or an investigation.
  8. Representation of private interests: This topic is too broad to cover completely here but there are several general rules to consider. First, you cannot represent others for compensation before the city except yourself or a relative. Generally, you cannot represent anyone before a group or staff that you work with but see the exceptions to this rule. Also, as an employee or official, you cannot represent someone in litigation that is adverse to the city.
  9. Conflicting outside employment: You cannot seek, accept, or engage in work that could reasonably be considered to impair independence of judgment in, or faithful performance of, official duties. Similarly, you cannot provide services for people or organizations that are requesting approval, an investigation, or a determination from the body or department where you are a member.
  10. Public property and resources: City workers and resources are not at your disposal to use for private or political purposes.
  11. Political activity: With a few exceptions, you must not use your position with the city on behalf of a political candidate, party, or committee. No one should attempt to influence subordinates about political activity, accept compensation for political activity related to items under your influence, or display campaign materials on public vehicles.
  12. Prohibited interests in contracts: You cannot have a direct or indirect financial interest in contracts or sales to the city. Similarly, you cannot individually or as the officer of an entity submit a bid or proposal or negotiate to enter into a city contract. You also may not participate in the making/awarding of city contracts where a party to the contract was also your employer within the last 12 months.
  13. Ex parte communications: If a matter is or reasonably may be pending before a quasi-judicial city board or commission regarding any adjudicative matter, you should not discuss the matter with city officials without a simultaneous and full disclosure of the communication.


Note that if your complaint or allegation does not directly relate to city officials, employees, and persons doing business with the city who are involved with fraud, waste, abuse, public corruption, or an ethics violation, you may need to contact another City of Dallas department, federal or state agency for assistance. The following matters are not generally investigated by the OIG: 911 emergencies, social security fraud, identity theft, tax fraud, phone scams, spam calls, Medicare/Medicaid fraud.
What do you need to file a complaint?
Our investigations are most successful when you provide as much information as possible about the allegation and those involved. The more you tell us, the better chance we have of determining whether an investigation can be pursued. Incomplete complaints may result in an investigation that cannot move forward due to a lack of information. To the greatest extent possible, the complaint should contain the following information and items:
  1. The name, address, email address, and telephone number of the complainant.
  2. The name, address (if known), email address (if known), and telephone number (if known) of each person who allegedly committed the violation (the respondent(s)).
  3. A statement of the facts on which the complaint is based, including the exact date or dates of the alleged violation.
  4. Identification of the ethics laws, ordinances, and rules allegedly violated, using either a citation to the applicable section or a description containing substantially the same language as the ethics laws, ordinances, and rules (if known).
  5. Copies of the documents or other evidence, if any, referenced in the complaint or in the complainant's possession that support the complaint attached to the complaint.
  6. The names, addresses, email addresses, and telephone numbers (if known) of witnesses, if any, that can offer testimony in support of the complaint.
  7. Other sources of evidence, if any, that the complainant recommends should be considered by the inspector general.
  8. An affidavit in which the complainant swears or affirms, under the penalty of perjury, that: 
    1. the complaint states a violation of this chapter;
    2. the complaint is not being presented for any improper purpose, such as to harass, cause unnecessary delays, or needlessly increase the cost of defense to the person charged in the complaint; and
    3. either: 
      1. all information submitted in and with the complaint is true and correct; or
      2. to the best of the complainant’s knowledge, the factual contentions in the complaint are supported by credible evidence submitted in and with the complaint.


What can I expect after submitting a complaint?
Once a complaint is submitted, it will be reviewed by our lead investigator.
What does confidentiality mean, and can my identity remain confidential and anonymous?
City officials and employees shall not reveal information relating to a complaint or investigation until it has been made public by the inspector general, except as required for the performance of official duties or as required by law. All papers and communications relating to a complaint or investigation are confidential unless required to be made public under the Public Information Act or other applicable law.
Can I remain anonymous?
The inspector general shall not accept, process, or investigate any complaint that is filed more than one year after the date of the alleged or suspected violation. This statute of limitations is tolled during any time an OIG investigation is halted due to ongoing criminal or civil litigation in accordance with Section 12A-47(b)(3).
What are the protections in place for whistleblowers and those who cooperate with investigations?
A whistleblower is a person who exposes any kind of information or activity that is deemed illegal, unethical, or not correct within an organization.
Pursuant to Section 12A-6 of the code, any person that files a complaint is protected. A person commits an offense if he or she retaliates against any person for filing a complaint, or for testifying, assisting, or participating in any manner in a proceeding or hearing. Additionally, the **Texas Whistleblowers Act (TWA)** protects public employees who make good faith reports of violations of law by their employer to an appropriate law enforcement authority. Under the TWA, an employer may not suspend or terminate the employment of, or take other adverse personnel action against, a public employee who makes a report. Examples of adverse actions may include termination, refusal to hire, denial of promotion, threats, unjustified negative evaluations or references, or increased surveillance.
Where can I find a recently issued or older quarterly report?
Confidentiality rules prevent the OIG from releasing your identity outside of our office in most cases. Similarly, the Code provides that no city official or employee shall reveal information relating to a complaint or investigation until it has been made public by the inspector general, except as required for the performance of official duties or as required by law.
The OIG will keep your identity confidential to the extent possible. However, in some cases, it may be necessary to share your complaint if we need to refer the matter to a more suitable department or to work with another governmental agency to investigate your complaint. Likewise, your identity may be revealed if it is the only way to effectively investigate an alleged matter or as otherwise required by law.
Be advised that:
  * Confidentiality only applies to your identity, not to the information provided (i.e., the OIG may, if necessary, share the facts and circumstances provided by you).
  * If you initially request anonymity, you can change your mind about that later. Your identity may be disclosed to other OIG personnel, prosecutors, and law enforcement agencies or pursuant to court order or additional requests.
  * Your identity will be included in OIG files, memoranda, records, etc.
  * The OIG cannot control whether other persons you reported allegations or information to will protect your identity.
  * Given the nature of the information you provide, other individuals, including coworkers, subordinates, supervisors, etc., may deduce or infer the OIG’s source of the information.


A request for anonymity may preclude the OIG from taking further action, if the information provided is of a specific or personal nature and that action would require identity disclosure (e.g., a person alleges retaliation).
Where can I find the status of my complaint?
To protect confidentiality and the investigative process, the OIG does not discuss details of any ongoing investigation. This is to protect and maintain the integrity of all investigations. The OIG generates summary reports of all cases each quarter. The OIG generates [summary reports](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/inspector-general/Pages/faq.aspx) of all cases each quarter.
How are complaints evaluated?
Initially, complaints are evaluated for viability. This means questions are addressed such as whether this matter is a violation of 12A or something else; will the OIG have jurisdiction over the compliant; is the complaint filed in time; can the complaint be proven before the Ethics Advisory Commission; etc. If these questions are answered in the affirmative, a full investigation will commence seeking to gather the facts needed to determine if the matter should be taken to a hearing, referred, or closed. It is possible that the complaint will be referred to another agency at any time in this process.
What is the difference between a preliminary and a full investigation?
A preliminary investigation is an informal inquiry into reported or discovered facts and circumstances to decide if they warrant a full investigation. The purpose of the preliminary inquiry is to determine if the questioned conduct occurred, and if so, whether it constitutes a violation, and ultimately, whether the matter should be investigated. A full investigation involves a close examination or a systematic inquiry of the alleged violations. This will usually involve interviews, document review, and the gathering of other evidence.
Will I be contacted during the investigation?
Yes. Receipt of the complaint will be acknowledged, and it is likely that you will be contacted with follow up questions. If you chose to remain anonymous, you will be contacted by email through the third-party complaint system.
Will documents and other evidence be gathered for the investigation?
All relevant information will be gathered. This may include witness testimony, audio/visual evidence, real evidence, and records of most every type. As a participant in the complaint process, you can anticipate being called on during the investigation to share what you know. If you are a city official, employee, or person doing business with the Code, you will have a duty to cooperate, be truthful, and avoid interference with the investigation.
Could my complaint be referred outside of the OIG?
The inspector general will receive and investigate all complaints. Once a complaint is received, the preliminary investigation phase commences. In this phase, the complaint’s foundational issues are addressed such as whether the matter is within the jurisdiction of the OIG, if there is enough information to investigate the matter, and if the matter should be referred elsewhere.
If the complaint meets these foundational requirements, it moves to the full investigation phase. In this phase, the inspector general can subpoena witnesses and compel the production of books, papers, and other evidence. Will I be informed if there is a referral?
Yes. A good example would be if the complaint is not a violation of the Ethics Code but is an HR issue such as habitually showing up late for work.
Which complaints will move to a hearing?
The inspector general can subpoena witnesses and compel the production of books, papers, and other evidence. Typically, witnesses will be interviewed, and documents and other relevant evidence will be gathered and inspected. The diagram below outlines the flow of cases during the phases of investigation.
Who will hear my complaint if it is found to be a credible allegation?
A complaint will move to a hearing if the inspector general determines that the violations can be proven by the legal standard of a “preponderance of the evidence.”
Can the complainant attend?
Complaints are heard by a five person panel. The panel is comprised of five members of the Ethics Advisory Commission that are randomly selected. At least two of the members of a panel must have a legal background.
How can a respondent defend themselves at the hearing?
Yes. People accused of a violation are referred to as respondents. Respondents may appear at the hearing with an advocate and make a statement, and present and cross-examine witnesses.
Can the respondent have an attorney present?
The hearing process for complaints begins when the inspector general files an information with the city secretary. The information lays out the allegations that will be brought to the hearing. At the same time, a copy of the information will be provided to the respondent. If possible, the hearing date is set about 45 days from the filing of the information.
At the hearing, the panel is not bound by the rules of evidence and may establish time limits and other rules relating to the participation of any person in the hearing. After hearing the evidence, the panel will decide whether the allegations are substantiated or not. A determination that a violation of this chapter has occurred can be made only upon a concurring vote of at least 3/4 of the members of a panel. 
The panel will make all reasonable efforts to issue a written decision within 20 days of the hearing. That decision will be forwarded to all parties and, if the panel finds that a violation occurred, that finding will be sent to the person or body responsible for discipline and sanctions. The range of outcomes are listed in Article X of the Code.
What are the requirements to find that a violation did occur?

What happens if the panel finds that a violation did occur?
If a violation is found to have occurred, the Ethics Advisory Commission will say it is substantiated and they will issue a written report. There is no appeal from this decision. From that point, the issue of discipline will be addressed by the panel in the form of a recommendation to the body with jurisdiction to decide what discipline or sanction is appropriate. In some cases, this is the city council, in other cases the issue is handled according to personnel rules and guidelines.
What is the make up of an Ethics Advisory Commission hearing panel?
The EAC hearing panel is comprised of five members of the Ethics Advisory Commission chosen at random by the city secretary at the time an information is filed. A quorum of four panel members must be present for a hearing.
What are some of the roles the OIG fulfills to help promote ethics and avoid fraud, waste, abuse, ethics violations, and corruption?
Respondents have the right to attend the hearing, make a statement, present, and cross-examine witnesses, and to represent themselves or be represented by legal counsel or another advisor. Respondents may retain an independent outside attorney from an approved list provided by the city attorney and the city will pay the reasonable and necessary fees from the time the complaint is filed through the conclusion of the hearing. Note that if a violation is substantiated (which means proven), the respondent must reimburse the city for the legal fees and expenses of their counsel.
