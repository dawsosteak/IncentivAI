​​​​Seniors
DCH Body
Page Content
​Older residents have unique needs when it comes to emergencies. Along with creating a network of neighbors, relatives, friends and co-workers to aid you in an emergency, Seniors should keep specialized items ready for an emergency. These items include extra wheelchair batteries, oxygen, catheters, medication, food for service animals, etc. 
​  

Keep a list of the type and model numbers of the medical devices you require. Be sure to make provisions for medications that require refrigeration. Make arrangements for any assistance to get to a shelter.
  

  * [Federal Benefits](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/Seniors.aspx#benefits)
  * [Create Support Network](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/Seniors.aspx#support)
  * [Med. Supplies](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/Seniors.aspx#supplies)
  * [Emergency Documents ](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/Seniors.aspx#documents)


A disaster can disrupt mail service for extended periods. Electronic​ payment is a simple, significant way seniors can protect themselves financially. 
The U.S. Department of the Treasury recommends two ways to receive federal benefits electronically:
  * **Direct deposit** to a checking or savings account is the best option for seniors with bank accounts. Federal benefit recipients can sign up by calling (800) 333-1795 or at [www.GoDirect.org](https://fiscal.treasury.gov/GoDirect/).
  * **The Direct Express® prepaid debit card** is a safe and easy alternative for seniors who don't have a bank account. Sign up is easy – call toll-free at (877) 212-9991 or sign up online at [www.USDirectExpress.com](https://www.usdirectexpress.com/). 

Signing up for direct deposit or the Direct Express® card is a simple step that can help protect your family's access to funds in an emergency.   
  

  * Create a network with family, friends, and others. Make a plan.
  * Write down and share ​​​your emergency plan with everyone in your support network.
  * Make sure everyone knows how you plan to evacuate your home or workplace and where you will go in case of a disaster.
  * Make sure that someone in your local network has an extra key to your home and knows where you keep your emergency supplies.
  * Teach those who will help you how to use any lifesaving equipment, administer medicine in case of an emergency.
  * Practice your plan with those who have agreed to be part of your network.​


  * If you take medicine or use a medical treatment on a daily basis, try to keep least a week's supply on hand.  

  * Establish a plan for rotating your supply of prescription or consumable medication so it remains up-to-date.  

  * Keep written copies of your prescriptions, over-the-counter medications and orders for medical equipment. Include dosage, treatment and allergy information in your emergency kit.  

  * Fill prescriptions on the first day you become eligible for a refill if you can't stock an emergency supply ​rather than waiting until the day you run out.  

  * If you undergo routine treatments administered by a clinic or hospital or if you receive regular services such as home health care, treatment or transportation, talk to your service provider about their emergency plans. Work with them to identify back-up service providers and incorporate them into your personal support network.  

  * ​Consider other personal needs such as eyeglasses, hearing aids and hearing aid batteries, wheelchair batteries and oxygen.  
  



  * ​Include copies of important documents in your emergency supply kits such as family records, medical records, wills, deeds, social security number, charge and bank accounts information and tax records.
  * Have copies of your medical insurance and Medicare cards readily available.
  * Keep a list of the style and serial number of medical devices or other life-sustaining devices. Include operating information and instructions.
  * Make sure that a friend or family member has copies of these documents.
  * Include the names and contact information of your support network, as well as your medical providers.
  * If you have a communication disability, make sure your emergency information notes the best way to communicate with you.
  * Keep these documents in a water proof container for quick and easy access.
  * Get and keep copies and maintain electronic versions of health records from doctors, hospitals, pharmacies and other sources and store them, for personal reference. A flash drive is a convenient way to stay up to date. HHS provides an online tool intended to help people locate and access their electronic health records from a variety of sources. [https://healthit.gov/bluebutton​](https://www.healthit.gov/topic/health-it-initiatives/blue-button)
