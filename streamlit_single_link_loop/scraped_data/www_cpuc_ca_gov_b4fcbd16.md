#  Building Decarbonization
## Building Decarbonization
“Building decarbonization” refers to the umbrella of methods to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from buildings. California buildings contribute approximately [24% of greenhouse gas emissions](https://efiling.energy.ca.gov/GetDocument.aspx?tn=241599) in the state. California has identified building decarbonization as a key strategy to meeting its long-term climate goals. 
The largest source of GHG emissions from buildings comes from consumption of natural gas and electricity. Additional smaller sources of greenhouse gas emissions from buildings include use of propane, kerosene, diesel, and wood (primarily in rural areas) and high global-warming-potential refrigerants that are used in refrigeration, cooling, and heat pump systems. The materials used to construct and maintain buildings also contribute significant GHG emissions over the lifetime of a building. This concept is referred to as _embodied carbon,_ defined by the California Energy Commission as the greenhouse gas emissions “resulting from the extraction, manufacturing, transportation, installation, maintenance, and disposal of building materials.” Decarbonizing buildings, therefore, means using strategies to address GHG emissions from the above sources.
The CPUC develops policies and oversees implementation of various program intended to encourage building decarbonization, notably including both the [TECH](https://techcleanca.com/) initiative and [BUILD](https://www.energy.ca.gov/programs-and-topics/programs/building-initiative-low-emissions-development-program-build) programs that were both created as a result of [SB 1477 (Stern, 2018)](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=201720180SB1477). More info regarding what the CPUC is doing to encourage building decarbonization can be found below.

  * Phase 1 addressed implementation of SB 1477. **[A.20-03-004](https://apps.cpuc.ca.gov/apex/f?p=401:56::::RP,57,RIR:P5_PROCEEDING_SELECT:A2003004):****Application of Southern California Edison Company (U338E) for Approval of its 2020 Energy Storage Procurement and Investment Plan.**
  * [D.22-04-044](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M472/K121/472121329.PDF), adopted April 21, 2022, approved, among other things, the implementation of SCE’s Smart HPHW program. Includes cost recovery of $13.9M to implement a program that will reach 17,000 customers and target 5 MW of behind-the-meter thermal energy storage by 2027.
  * Similar to PG&E’s WatterSaver program, SCE’s Smart HPHW program includes incentives for the replacement of natural gas water heaters with electric heat pump water heaters.


**[I.22-09-011](https://apps.cpuc.ca.gov/apex/f?p=401:56:0::NO:RP,57,RIR:P5_PROCEEDING_SELECT:I2209011):****Investigation to Address the Potential Loss of Natural Gas Service for a Portion of the Santa Nella Community**
  * [D.23-04-057](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M507/K498/507498093.PDF) adopted April 27, 2023, directs PG&E to install new electric and gas distribution infrastructure for a portion of the Santa Nella community in Merced County, and also gives residents the option to forgo gas service and fully electrify their homes at no cost.”  



**[R.15-03-010](https://apps.cpuc.ca.gov/apex/f?p=401:56:0::NO:RP,57,RIR:P5_PROCEEDING_SELECT:R1503010):****Rulemaking to Identify Disadvantaged Communities in the San Joaquin Valley and Analyze Economically Feasible Options to Increase Access to Affordable Energy in those Disadvantaged Communities**
  * [D.17-05-014](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M187/K072/187072828.pdf), adopted May 11, 2017, adopts a methodology for identifying San Joaquin Valley (SJV) disadvantaged communities (DACs) eligible under Public Utilities Code Section 783.5. 
  * [D.18-08-019](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/SearchRes.aspx?DocFormat=ALL&DocID=225574950), adopted August 23, 2018, approves a data gathering plan to gather baseline data on energy, household, and community conditions within SJV DACs. The decision also adds nine communities to the list of SJV DACs and establishes a process for changes to the list.
  * [D.18-12-015](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M252/K522/252522682.PDF), adopted December 20, 2018, approves 12 pilot projects across 11 communities in the San Joaquin Valley; 11 of these pilots converted homes to all-electric end uses, allowing residents to move away from propane and wood for heating and cooking.
  * [D.19-02-030](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M260/K336/260336304.PDF), adopted February 25, 2019, corrects several errors in D.18-12-015.


  * [D.20-04-006](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M334/K576/334576899.PDF), adopted April 15, 2020, modifies D.18-12-015 and removes income eligibility requirements for four of the communities, making all residents in these communities eligible for full participation the SJV DAC pilot. The decision also directs PG&E and SCE to implement a two-way balancing account to track the costs of implementing the bill protection and transitional community solar discount authorized in [Resolution E-5034](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M322/K776/322776695.docx).

  * [D.25-06-068](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M571/K402/571402218.PDF) was adopted on June 27, 2025, dismissing [A.24-07-002](https://apps.cpuc.ca.gov/apex/f?p=401:56::::::).
  * Application filed by PG&E on behalf of customers seeking a gas line subsidy pursuant to requirements of decision [D.22-09-026](https://docs.cpuc.ca.gov/PublishedDocs/Published/G000/M496/K987/496987290.PDF). The decision directed utilities to "file an application with the Commission, on behalf of applicants, for approval of a gas line subsidy" which meets certain criteria.
