#  Drinking Water Disinfection - Chlorine & Chloramine 
Water systems generally use multiple barriers to prevent contamination of their water systems. Like other water systems, Anaheim: 
  * has programs in place to prevent backflows (the flow of water from a customer’s facility into public water system)
  * maintains a tight water distribution system
  * tests its water regularly
  * utilizes high quality source water


These precautions to keep microorganisms out of our water system, but it is still possible for them to sneak in from an unforeseen event such as a pipeline break or inadvertent backflow. Anaheim supplements these protections by adding disinfectants to help keep the public water supply safe.
## Chlorine & Chloramine
Chlorine and chloramine are common chemicals used for disinfecting the water supply. 
Although the words “chlorine” and “chloramine” sound similar, they aren’t interchangeable. Chloramine is group of chemical compounds that contain chlorine and ammonia, while chlorine contains no other chemicals. 
In Anaheim, chlorine is added to well water and at water treatment facilities to ensure the water remains bacteria-free. Disinfectant levels are tested weekly at 51 locations. 
The Metropolitan Water District of Southern California (MWD), Southern California’s regional water wholesaler, adds chloramine at their treatment plants. 
Water from MWD is supplied to Anaheim through pipes located throughout the city. The amount of water supplied by MWD varies depending on Anaheim’s water demand and the availability of other sources of supply. In most cases, MWD water, which uses chloramine, makes up less than 15% of Anaheim’s total water supply.
It’s important to note that chlorine and chloramine are important to ensure drinking water is safe, but it can be a concern for kidney dialysis procedures.
## A Matter of Taste
Water containing chlorine can taste and smell different than untreated water or water containing chloramine. To change the taste of your water with chlorine, there are a few methods you can use to remove or neutralize the chlorine taste. 
### Cooling
Chlorine can be removed by leaving an open container of tap water in your fridge. The cooling process can take 24 hours or more, depending on the amount of water. During this time, the chlorine will evaporate. 
### Sitting
Another chlorine removal technique is to leave your drinking water sitting at room temperature. While this process can also take 24 hours or more, it can sometimes be faster than putting your water in the fridge.
