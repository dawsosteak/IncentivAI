DCH Body
​**The Good, the Bad and the Ugly**

Page Content
The definition of an invasive species is: 
1) A species that is non-native to the ecosystem under consideration and 2) whose introduction causes or is likely to cause economic or environmental harm or harm to human health. Invasive species can be plants, animals and other organisms (e.g., microbes).
Below are just a few common invasive species in our area, and some control methods. If chemical treatment is needed, please check with your local landscape or tree service for a licensed applicator.

Emerald Ash Borer (EAB)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/home/tiles-invasive-species.jpg)
  
**Public Alert - May 2024**  

Please be advised that the presence of the invasive emerald ash borer (EAB) has been confirmed in the Dallas city limits. 
[Latest Updates](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/Pages/latest-news.aspx)  

  

**What is the emerald ash borer?**
The emerald ash borer (Agrilus planipennis) is a destructive, non‐native, wood‐boring pest of ash trees (Fraxinus spp.). Native to Asia, the emerald ash borer beetle (EAB) was unknown in North America until its discovery in southeast Michigan in 2002. All native ash species are susceptible to attack. Ash trees with low population densities of EAB often have few or no external symptoms of infestation. EAB is a significant threat to urban, suburban and rural forests as it kills both stressed and healthy ash trees. EAB is very aggressive and ash trees may die within two or three years after they become infested. 
  

**Learn More**   

Through the "Emerald Ash Borer" links on this site you can visit the [EAB Information Center](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/Pages/publications-resources.aspx) for downloadable materials, documents, videos and links to additional resources. Also, review the answers to Frequently Asked Questions, learn how to identify ash trees that may be infested and learn about programs to help replace ash trees with more suitable tree species.

Chinaberry (Melia azedarach)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/Invasive-Species/Chinaberry1.jpg)
  

  * Introduced in the mid-1800s from Asia
  * Deciduous tree
  * Lavender flowers in spring
  * Outcompetes native vegetation
  * Reproduces primarily from root sprouts (via birds too)


The most effective chemical controls are cut-stump and basal bark applications of triclopyr herbicidices. Cut trees left untreated will grow back with several branches emanating from a single stump. Removal of seedlings must include the entire root system.  

  
  

​  

Chinese privet (Ligustrum sinense)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/Invasive-Species/Slide2.JPG)
  * Introduced in early 1852   

  * Aggressive and troublesome
  * Forms dense thickets  

  * Will transform native habitats into monospecific (single species), not allowing any other species to grow  

  * Shade tolerant
  * Colonizes by root sprouts and spreads widely by abundant bird and other animals despersing seeds


Control: Most effective is to cut down, leaving 12 " stump, apply herbicide immediately and directly to the fresh cut. ​Prolific seeder, so constant mowing will be needed to keep it from growing.
Tree of heaven (Ailanthus altissima)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/Invasive-Species/Slide4.JPG)
  

  * First introduced to America by a gardener in Philadelphia, PA in 1784
  * Large compound leaves, 1-4 feet in length
  * 11-25 smaller leaflets that alternate along the stem
  * All part of the tree, especially the flowers. Have a strong offensive odor  



  

Control: Most effective is to cut down, leaving 12 " stump, apply herbicide immediately and directly to the fresh cut.  

  

Chinese tallow (Triadica sebifera)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/Invasive-Species/Slide6.JPG)
  * Introduced into the US in the 1700s in South Carolina
  * Propagates via cuttings, stumps and roots
  * Will transform native habitats into monospecific (single species) tallow forests


Control: Apply a triclopyr herbicide to the basal bark in late summer or early fall, or for large trees, apply directy to the stump after cutting down. Pull up seedlings or in large areas can be managed by mowing.  

  

​  

Giant reed (Arundo donax)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/PublishingImages/Pages/Invasive-Species/Slide8.JPG)
  * Introduced into the US in the early 1800's in Los Angeles, CA  

  * Giant reed chokes rivers, streams and channels  

  * Crowds out native plants, interferes with flood control, increases fire potential and reduces habitat for wildlife
  * Reproduction is primarily vegetative, through rhizomes which root and sprout readily
  * Fragment can float downstream and take root


  

Control: Systemic herbicides may be applied to clumps of giant reed, after flowering, either as a cut stump treatment or as a foliar spray. Mechanical control, such as repeated mowing, may be somewhat effective but may leave fragments of roots in the soils which will then regrow.  

  

Feral Hogs (Sus scrofa)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/Pages/feral%20hog.jfif)
  

​Feral hogs are an Old World species and the most reproductively efficient animal on Earth. They can adapt to survive in nearly any environment. Statisticians have estimated that due to the feral hog's high reproduction rate, 70% to 75% of the population must be harvested to control the population. In 2007, researchers estimated that each pig carried an associated (damage plus control) cost of $300 per year and, with an estimated 5 million wild pigs in the population at the time, Americans are spending over $1.5 billion annually in damages and control costs. Assuming that the cost-per-wild pig estimate has remained constant, the annual costs in the United States are likely closer to $2.1 billion today. 
  

Texas Invasives
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/Pages/soda_apple.jpg)
What is a noxious and invasive plant?  

Any plant species that has a serious potential to cause economical or ecological harm to the agriculture, horticulture, native plants, ecology and waterways of Texas.
  

Nutria (Myocastor coypus)
![](https://dallascityhall.com/projects/forestry/Pages/nutria.jpg)
​A large, herbivorous, semiaquatic rodent native to South America. Besides breeding quickly, each nutria consumes large amounts of aquatic vegetation. An individual consumes about 25% of its body weight daily, and feeds year-round. [31][40] Being one of the world's larger extant rodents, a mature, healthy nutria averages 11 lbs in weight, but they can reach as much as 22 lbs. They eat the base of the above-ground stems of plants and often dig through soil for roots and rhizomes to eat.  

Nutrias are considered invasive in the United States due to their destructive burrowing and feeding habits.
