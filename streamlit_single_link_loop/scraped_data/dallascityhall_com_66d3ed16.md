​​Pets
DCH Body
Page Content
​Millions of pet owners consider their pet as an important member of their household. Make a plan and a kit for your pets as you would any other family member. Keep in mind that what's best for you is typically what's best for your animals.
  

**DO NOT LEAVE YOUR PETS BEHIND!**
Plan in advance for shelter alternatives that will work for both you and your pets; consider loved ones or friends outside of your immediate area who would be willing to host you and your pets in an emergency. Not all public shelters are equipped or allow pets. Know before you go. 
​​  

​Make a back-up emergency plan in case you can't care for your animals yourself. Develop a buddy system with neighbors, friends and relatives to make sure that someone is available to care for or evacuate your pets if you are unable to do so.
​  

  * [Plan](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/pets.aspx#plan)
  * [Large Animals](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/pets.aspx#large)
  * [More Information](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/officeemergencymanagement/Pages/pets.aspx#more)


Plan for pet needs during a disaster by:
  * **Identifying shelter.** For public health reasons, many emergency shelters cannot accept pets. There are a number of guides that list hotels/motels that permit pets and could serve as a starting point. Your local animal shelter might be able to provide information concerning pets during a disaster.
  * Before you find yourself in an emergency situation, pack a "pet survival" kit. Include: pet food, bottled water, medications, veterinary records, cat litter/pan, manual can opener, food dishes, and other supplies with you. 
  * Make sure identification tags and chip information are up-to-date and tags are securely fastened to your pet's collar. If possible, attach the address and/or phone number of your evacuation site. If your pet gets lost, his tag and chip are his ticket home.
  * Have a current photo of your pet for identification purposes.
  * Have a secure pet carrier, leash or harness for your pet so that if he panics, he can't escape.  


Take the following steps to prepare to shelter your pet:  

  * Call your local emergency management office, animal shelter or animal control office to get advice and information.  

  * Identify boarding facilities. Include those outside your local area in case local facilities close and you are unable to return home right away. 
  * Most boarding kennels, veterinarians,​ and animal shelters will need your pet's medical records to make sure all vaccinations are current. Include copies in your "pet survival" kit along with a photo of your pet.
  * Some animal shelters will provide temporary foster care for owned pets in times of disaster but this should be considered only as a last resort.
  * Leave your pets at home only as an extreme last resort and if there is no other alternative. Leaving your pet at home alone can place your animal in great danger! Confine your pet to a safe area inside - _NEVER_ leave your pet outside! Leave them loose inside your home with food and plenty of water. Remove the toilet tank lid, raise the seat and brace the bathroom door open so they can drink. Place a notice outside in a visible area, advising where pets are in the house. Provide a phone number where you or a contact can be reached as well as the name and number of your vet.


Protect your pet during a disaster:​
  * Bring your pets inside immediately.
  * Have newspapers on hand for sanitary purposes. Feed the animal moist or canned food so they will need less water to drink.
  * Animals have instincts about severe weather changes and will often isolate themselves if they are afraid. Bringing them inside early can stop them from running away. Never leave a pet outside or tied up during a storm.
  * Separate dogs and cats. Even if your dogs and cats normally get along, the anxiety of an emergency situation can cause pets to act irrationally. Keep small pets away from cats and dogs.
  * In an emergency, you may have to take your birds with you. Talk with your veterinarian or local pet store about special food dispensers that regulate the amount of food a bird is given. Make sure that the bird is caged and the cage is covered by a thin cloth or sheet to provide security and filtered light.


If you have large animals such as horses, cattle, sheep, goats or pigs on your property, take precautions before a disaster.​
  * Ensure all animals have some form of identification.
  * Evacuate animals whenever possible. Map out primary and secondary routes in advance.
  * Make available vehicles and trailers needed for transporting and supporting each type of animal. Also make available experienced handlers and drivers. Note: It is best to allow animals a chance to become accustomed to vehicular travel so they are less frightened and easier to move.
  * Ensure destinations have food, water, veterinary care and handling equipment.
  * If evacuation is not possible, animal owners must decide whether to move large animals to shelter or turn them outside.
