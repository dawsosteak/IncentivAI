Mike Specian  Washington, DC, CA20045 The American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy (ACEEE) submits this letter in support of the Petition for Modification of Decision D.19-05-019. ACEEE strongly supports California’s clean energy and decarbonization goals, and its demonstrated interest in using cost-effective energy efficiency (EE) for those ends. However, the most effective EE measures have evolved over the last decade. Previously, the efficient lighting was one of the most successful EE measures, delivering high savings at low cost. But as the lighting market has matured, efficient LED technology is now ubiquitous, diminishing the need for utility programs to support them. ACEEE research finds that the most valuable EE measures for enabling a low-cost, high renewable energy future are those that address heating and cooling needs in buildings, primarily electric heat pumps, weatherization, and water heating. The critical distinctions between these measures and the valuable EE measures of the past are 1) these measures are long-lived, delivering savings potentially for decades and 2) they often have higher up-front costs. High up-front cost remains one of the primary impediments to efficient technology purchases, and a key reason why utility EE programs remain so crucial for their adoption. When the CPUC issued D.19-05-019, it ordered that the Total Resource Cost (TRC) test would be the primary test for all Commission activities. Determining the cost-effectiveness of an EE approach requires adopting a perspective (e.g., utility, program participants, society). This is why different cost-effectiveness tests were established by the California Standard Practice Manual many years ago, and have been used throughout the U.S. and Canada since. Because these tests all answer different questions, the recommended best practice is to evaluate multiple tests in combination, favoring the tests that make the most sense in the context of the EE measure being considered. In the current context, the TRC test is not the most appropriate for evaluating California’s evolving EE needs, largely due to the TRC’s inclusion of participant costs. Even though technologies like heat pumps and weatherization deliver substantial benefits like long-term affordability, decarbonization, and grid reliability their high up-front costs to participants can cause these measures to fail the TRC test. However, a participant’s willingness to pay higher up-front costs for efficiency improvements that will deliver long-lived savings as well as other benefits such as improved comfort should not serve as an argument against the value of those programs. The consequence of the TRC test is that valuable efficiency measures are less likely to be incentivized by program administrators. This lowers the chances that these measures will be adopted by ratepayers, keeping them from contributing to state goals. A more appropriate cost-effectiveness screen for the purposes of program budget allocation—and one that we recommend the CPUC adopt—is the Program Administrator Cost (PAC) test. The PAC test reflects the cost to distributed energy resource (DER) program administrators and the true cost to ratepayers. The PAC test is better aligned with California’s clean energy, affordability, decarbonization, and reliability priorities, including its goal of deploying 6 million heat pumps by 2030. Moving from the TRC test to the PAC test for program budget allocation decisions will unlock greater private sector investment in DERs by removing the disincentive of counting participant costs. In conclusion, ACEEE supports the Petition for Modification of D.19-05-019. We agree that the TRC test inappropriately penalizes the measures California needs most to meet its climate and energy goals and agree it is preferable to utilize the PAC test for DER program budget allocation decisions.
