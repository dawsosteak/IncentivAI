B. Comment Period: Was the 30-day comment period waived (see Rule 14.6(c)(6))? No If not: Party: Clean Coalition CPUC Discussion Clean Coalition’s (Coalition) comments (Comments) to the Alternate Proposed Decision (APD) allege errors of law and fact. We have reviewed the Comments and have not found valid information and arguments that would support these allegations. One of many defects of the Comments is their failure to provide correct and specific references to the record, which violates the mandate of Rule 14.3(c) of the Commission Rules of Practice and Procedure: Comments shall focus on factual, legal or technical errors in the proposed or alternate decision and in citing such errors shall make specific references to the record or applicable law. Comments that which fail to do so will be accorded no weight. We have thoroughly checked every argument in the Comments on the intervenor compensation related rulings, decisions, and the APD and found that the Coalition misreads, misquotes, misrepresents, misinterprets and makes arbitrary assumptions about, all these documents. In large part, the accusatory tone of the Comments rests upon these defects. For example, Coalition attempts to present a denial of the compensation as an attack on the renewable energy markets. This and similar attempts are also, in part, a result of the misconception regarding the Intervenor Compensation Program. The Coalition asserts its entitlement to the compensation award as a matter of policy; however, the intervenor compensation award is not a public purpose grant for a development of the renewable energy. The purpose of this Program is to give a voice in our proceedings to the underrepresented interests – the interests of the ratepayers who have financial barriers to their participation before this agency. Unlike these ratepayers, the industry and markets to which the Coalition provides its services have financial incentives to advance their views. The APD does not create new rules for the intervenors, but, based on the facts and statements provided by the Coalition and on the applicable law, determines that this group is not eligible as an organization representing the interests (including environmental concerns) of residential ratepayers, as Section 1802(b)(1)(C) requires. The Coalition has not provided valid information or reason that would support a reversal of this determination. The Coalition’s attempt to picture itself as an eligible group is also not supported by specific references to the record. Without such references, the Comments do not and cannot address a gap between the facts describing this group as a non-profit providing services to the renewable energy industry and market and the group’s declaratory statements that it single-mindedly

Finally, the Comments claim that it is unfair to apply retroactively our findings of the Coalition’s ineligibility, because the Coalition participated in reliance on the decisions that awarded compensation. While there were decisions awarding compensation to the Coalition, they mostly relied on the July 19, 2011 Ruling. An effect of that ruling has long expired. When the Ruling of June 30, 2016 found that the Coalition was not eligible, it was a result of the long and thorough investigation by the Commission of the Coalition’s activities, the investigation that launched with the Ruling of March 3, 2015. As any intervenor, the Coalition was to act prudently and reasonably in incurring costs of participation, given the investigation and the uncertainty with the Coalition’s standing. More important, we could not find any legal basis for granting compensation to an intervenor that is not eligible for such compensation. The Commission’s past practice contains no decision that would conclude that an ineligible intervenor should receive compensation because it was participating without a warning about its ineligibility. When a decision found an intervenor ineligible, no compensation was awarded.8 We continue to follow this practice.
