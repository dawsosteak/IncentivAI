## Addressing PFAS
At Anaheim Public Utilities, we provide **high quality water** for everyone in Anaheim.
We are **taking a leading role in addressing PFAS** , industrial chemicals that have been found in water sources across our region, state and the nation.
We have put in place and continue to expand **advanced treatment systems** for our groundwater wells with a goal of getting to undetectable levels of PFAS.
The treatment systems, shown here at our Linda Vista Complex, are based on **ion exchange** , a **filtering system** that uses resin beads to grab and remove particles from water. 
Ion exchange is an effective way to remove PFAS and also filters other particles to **make our water even cleaner**.![Tustin for web](https://www.anaheim.net/ImageRepository/Document?documentId=54401)
The treatment systems, made up of filters, tanks, control valves, pumps and pipes, are helping us meet or exceed **state and federal regulations** for PFAS in groundwater.
You can learn more about our**$200 million groundwater treatment program** [**here**](https://www.anaheim.net/5817/Groundwater-Treatment-Program).
Anaheim’s groundwater supply is an **incredible natural resource** that provides cost-effective water for our city. Today, treated wells provide **more than three-quarters of the water used by our residents and businesses.**
The treatment systems are helping us keep water**costs low** for our customers while also providing **great drinking water** for Anaheim. 
## About PFAS
**PFAS** are a family of industrial chemical compounds that have been in use in the United States since the 1940s. PFAS stands for per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances. 
Two of the most notable PFAS compounds that have been detected locally are PFOA and PFOS. These particular industrial chemicals are no longer used in the U.S.**PFOA** stands for perfluorooctanoic acid, while **PFOS** is perfluorooctane sulfonate.
You'll also see **other PFAS compounds** , including PFNA, PFHxS and HFPO-DA.
## Uses
Dating back to the 1940s, PFOA and PFOS have been **commonly used** to coat carpets, clothes, furniture, food packaging, cookware and other products.
We’ve all encountered PFOA and PFOS in our**daily lives**.![single drop](https://www.anaheim.net/ImageRepository/Document?documentId=26545)
They were **popular** for their resistance to water, stains or sticking, which made them particularly useful in cookware.
They were also used in **fire-retardant foams, cleaning products and industrial** uses.
## Phase-out
In the 2000s, manufacturers began **phasing out** use of PFOA and PFOS under a federal Environmental Protection Agency program.
PFOA and PFOS are **no longer produced** in the United States.
They still are produced in and imported from other countries but are being phased out, with some exemptions, under**a ban** put forth in 2019 under the Stockholm Convention, an international treaty covering pollutants.
Manufacturers have developed **other PFAS compounds** over the years as a replacement for PFOA and PFOS.
## What’s the issue?
Scientific studies have shown that long-term exposure to PFAS compounds at high levels can cause **health issues**.
The **federal Environmental Protection Agency** , the lead agency for protecting drinking water nationally, has set **limits on five PFAS compounds** in drinking water. 
For both **PFOA and PFOS, the limit is 4 parts per trillion**. 
For **PFNA, PFHxS and HFPO-DA** , the limit is **10 parts per trillion**.
Parts per million, billion or trillion are ways to **measure tiny amounts** of something in water or air.
It is a **microscopic** measurement: 1 part per trillion is comparable to four grains of sugar in an Olympic-size swimming pool.
New federal PFAS limits go into effect i**n 2029**.
In **California** , the state Division of Drinking Water monitors and enforces federal guidelines.
**California** also has what are known as **a “notification level” and a “response level”** for water agencies encountering specific PFAS compounds including PFOA, PFOS, PFBS and PFHxS.
A **notification level** requires water agencies to notify government officials when a measure exceeds the notification level.
You can see Anaheim's notifications for [2019](https://www.anaheim.net/DocumentCenter/View/26534/PFOA-PFOS-notification-June-2019), [2023](https://www.anaheim.net/DocumentCenter/View/69698/PFOA-PFOS-Notification-March-2023), and [2026](https://www.anaheim.net/DocumentCenter/View/69741/Notification-Concerning-Detection-of-PFAS-2026_FINAL).
California’s response level requires water agencies to take action for **PFAS compounds at readings** of more than 10 parts per trillion for PFOA, 40 parts per trillion for PFOS, 5,000 parts per trillion for PFBS and 20 parts per trillion for PFHxS.
## PFAS in Anaheim
At Anaheim Public Utilities, we are **closely monitoring** evolving state and federal regulations and will always meet or exceed them. 
We regularly test groundwater wells and monitor PFAS levels. 
We took **some****groundwater wells** out of service in response to PFAS levels as we implemented treatment systems. Wells with treatment are now back in service and provide local groundwater that meets or exceeds local, state and federal standards.
Our **remaining groundwater wells meet or exceed** current state guidelines, and we plan to install PFAS treatment systems for those to meet or exceed pending federal guidelines.
In all, **wells supply more than 80 percent of Anaheim’s water** needs with more costly imported river water making up the rest.
## Why we’re seeing PFAS
Only recently has technology advanced to **detect tiny traces of PFAS** compounds. 
In Orange County, what we are encountering is believed to be **remnant** **traces** from consumer products and factories, military bases and other industrial uses all around us in Southern California.
The chemicals can make their way into rivers, such as the **Santa Ana River** , which replenishes much of Orange County’s groundwater wells.
You can have confidence that Anaheim’s water is thoroughly treated and tested to ensure it meets some of the**highest standards in the world**.
