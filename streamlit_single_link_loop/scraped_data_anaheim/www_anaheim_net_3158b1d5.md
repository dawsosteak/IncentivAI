# Living with Coyotes
  

Coyotes are common across Anaheim.
While you might never see a coyote, you should know what to do if you do see one in our neighborhoods, parks and other places.
Anaheim has a management plan for coyotes.
The plan relies on the only proven, long-term approach to managing coyotes: deterrence.
Trying to eliminate coyotes doesn’t work. When a coyote is taken out of one area, other coyotes move in. Coyotes, which are highly adaptable, also breed more to offset losses.
Living with coyotes through deterrence is what we all need to do.
  

You can see our Coyote Management Plan by clicking [here](https://ca-anaheim.civicplus.com/DocumentCenter/View/16816/Coyote-Management-Plan?bidId=). 
# What You Can Do
Coyotes are drawn to neighborhoods for easy sources of food, including trash cans and outdoor pet food, which draw small rodents and other animals that coyotes feed on.
There are easy steps you can take to make your home and neighborhood uninviting to coyotes and to keep your home, family and pets safe.
## Feeding
Don’t feed coyotes or other wildlife.Feeding wildlife can be a violation of the city’s municipal code and result in fines.
There can be some exceptions for feral cats. But know that you can be creating a potentially dangerous situation for feral cats if coyotes are attracted to feeding.
To be clear: Never leave food for coyotes. That leads the animals to associate people with food and to lose all natural fear of humans. We need to always work to responsibly re-instill in coyotes a natural fear of people.
## Hazing
If you encounter a coyote, stand tall, wave your arms and yell.
This is what is known as hazing and is for any wildlife you may encounter.
Do not run, turn your back or lose eye contact.
If necessary, throw rocks near, not at, a coyote, always being cautious about bending down or losing direct visual contact. 
Sadly, some coyotes are habituated, or too comfortable around people. Always seek to responsibly haze, but if a coyote doesn’t respond, slowly walk away backward maintaining eye contact, a tall posture and yelling if necessary.
## Walking Dogs
Use a leash that provides good control of your dog — retractable leashes are not recommended.
When possible, walk your dog with another person.
Consider carrying a stick or other defensive item.
Mace or other legal self-defense sprays are not recommended, but if you consider them know they can blow back and impact you as well as a coyote.
Should you encounter a coyote, pick up your dog if possible or place behind you.
Then stand tall, yell or throw something toward the coyote. Don't run — you'll trigger the coyote’s predator instinct.
## Coyote Deterrents and Tips
Supervise children and pets at all times outside.
Remove sources of drinking water.
Don't leave your pet's food dish out at night, especially with food still in it.
Don't leave pet cats or small dogs outside at night unless they are in a roofed kennel with an animal proof lock.
The best way to keep coyotes away from your backyard is to install a 6-foot fence with roller-bars on the top.
This will typically keep family members and pets safe from prowling coyotes, though occasionally large coyotes can clear even 6-foot fences.
  

Coyotes tend to hunt at night, so motion sensor lights can help deter them, again keeping in mind coyotes are adaptable and can become used to their settings.
Keep yard bushes trimmed to prevent coyotes from having a place to hide.
  

Watch for and ensure your yard has no convenient den spots.
## Reporting Coyote Sightings
We encourage you to report coyote sightings. 
Reports help us track coyote activity and give a better idea of where we are seeing the most encounters.
While coyote incidents with people are rare, California and county regulations require anyone to report a bite or scratch from a wild animal. 
Report an animal bite or scratch from a domestic or wild animal by calling the county of Orange Rabies Control Desk at (714) 796-6421 or visiting [OCPetinfo.com](https://ocpetinfo.com/).
